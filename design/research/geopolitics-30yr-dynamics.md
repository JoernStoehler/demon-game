# Geopolitical Dynamics Over 30-Year Timescales

Research for card content: what mechanisms and repeating patterns would a game about maintaining a global AI pause treaty need to capture?

---

## 1. Treaty Regime Evolution Over Decades

### What Determines Whether a Treaty Strengthens or Weakens?

**Strengthening factors (Montreal Protocol model):**
- Available technological alternatives (industry can comply without economic suicide)
- Multilateral Fund paying developing countries' compliance costs ($3B+ disbursed)
- Adaptive amendments responding to new science (London 1990, Copenhagen 1992, Kigali 2016)
- Scientific assessment panels giving apolitical evidence base for tightening
- Universal membership creating social pressure (198 parties — first universally ratified UN treaty)
- Entrepreneurial leadership by secretariat heads (UNEP's Tolba was pivotal early on)

**Weakening factors (NPT model):**
- Grand bargain erosion: nuclear states promised disarmament but modernize arsenals instead
- States outside the regime building capabilities anyway (India, Pakistan, Israel, North Korea)
- Review conferences failing to reach consensus (2015, 2022)
- Bilateral deals undermining multilateral norms (US-India nuclear deal, China-Pakistan cooperation)
- Geopolitical rifts making consensus impossible (Russia blocking 2022 NPT review)

**Key insight for the game:** Treaties don't just persist or collapse — they enter a zombie state where the institution exists but can't adapt. The NPT still has near-universal membership but hasn't produced a consensus outcome document since 2010. The game should model this: the treaty can be "alive" but increasingly hollow.

### How Do New Technologies Challenge Existing Frameworks?

The CWC faced this directly. Syria joined in 2013 under international pressure, then used chemical weapons 50+ times while a member. The OPCW had to develop entirely new investigation mechanisms (the Investigation and Identification Team) that weren't in the original treaty — a years-long political fight. The CWC's declaration-and-verification model assumed good-faith initial declarations; Syria submitted an incomplete one and then stonewalled inspectors for years.

The CTBT built an impressive International Monitoring System (321 stations, 16 labs, ~90% operational) that successfully detected all six North Korean tests. But the treaty itself never entered into force because key states (US, China, India, Pakistan, North Korea) haven't ratified. The verification infrastructure works; the political commitment doesn't exist.

**Game mechanism:** Verification technology can advance while political will erodes. These are independent variables.

### How Do "Insincere Members" Behave?

Patterns from NPT, CWC, and export control regimes:

1. **Sign to avoid sanctions/isolation, violate secretly.** Syria joined the CWC under threat of military strikes, then kept undeclared stockpiles and used them. Iraq maintained secret programs under IAEA safeguards for years.

2. **Exploit declaration ambiguity.** States submit technically truthful but misleading declarations. Syria's "initial declaration" was incomplete by design — they declared some facilities to appear compliant while hiding others.

3. **Use the treaty for intelligence.** Membership gives access to information about other states' programs, inspection methodologies, and detection thresholds. Knowing what inspectors look for helps you hide.

4. **Block institutional responses.** Russia used its position in the OPCW to resist attribution findings about Syria's chemical weapons use. Insincere members don't just violate — they capture enforcement mechanisms from the inside.

5. **Withdraw when caught.** North Korea announced withdrawal from the NPT in 2003 when its secret enrichment program was discovered.

**Game mechanism:** Insincere members are more dangerous inside the treaty than outside it. Detecting them costs intel resources; confronting them costs leverage.

## 2. Great Power Dynamics and Technology Control

### How COCOM Worked — and How It Was Evaded

COCOM (1949-1994) coordinated Western export controls against the Soviet bloc. Key lessons:

**What worked:**
- Controls on large, hard-to-conceal equipment (e.g., semiconductor fabrication tools, precision machine tools)
- Chokepoint targeting: focusing on items with few suppliers and no easy substitutes
- Intelligence-driven enforcement: monitoring known procurement networks

**What failed:**
- **Thousands of Soviet agents** deployed as "technology collection officers" across Western Europe and the US
- **Shell companies and front organizations** purchased restricted items through chains of intermediaries
- **Toshiba-Kongsberg scandal (1987):** Japanese and Norwegian companies sold 9-axis CNC milling machines to the USSR by falsely declaring them as simpler 2-axis machines. This let the Soviets manufacture dramatically quieter submarine propellers, reportedly making their subs 20x harder to detect. Discovered only because a whistleblower (former Toshiba sales manager) reported it to COCOM.
- **Ally compliance was inconsistent.** Members had economic incentives to sell. Enforcement depended on each nation's export control authorities, which varied in competence and motivation.
- **Once technology left the manufacturer, tracking was nearly impossible.** End-user certificates were routinely falsified.

**Game mechanism:** Technology denial works best against large, specialized equipment (chokepoints) and worst against small, fungible items. Allies have economic incentives to defect. Whistleblowers are a critical detection mechanism.

### The A.Q. Khan Network: Proliferation Entrepreneurship

Khan ran a nuclear smuggling network for 12+ years across 20+ countries, netting $100M+ from Libya alone. Key features:

- **Shell companies** (sometimes just a fax machine and empty office in Dubai) handled procurement and shipping
- **Distributed manufacturing**: centrifuge components built in Malaysia, South Africa, Turkey
- **Complete packages available**: blueprints, components, technical expertise — a menu with prices from millions to hundreds of millions
- **Exploited lax export controls** in Switzerland, Germany, Netherlands, UK
- **Provided 2,000+ centrifuge components to Iran** over years before discovery

**Game mechanism:** Once expertise exists, it can be packaged and sold. Non-state proliferation networks are harder to stop than state programs because they're distributed and commercially motivated.

### US-China Technology Competition: Current Trajectory

The US semiconductor export controls (October 2022, expanded March 2025) reveal the fundamental tension in technology denial:

- **Chokepoint strategy works in the short term**: chipmaking equipment is large, heavy, produced by few suppliers (ASML, Applied Materials, etc.), and hard to install/maintain secretly
- **But controls accelerate indigenous development**: China launched an "all-out, government-backed effort" for semiconductor self-sufficiency in response
- **US companies lose revenue and R&D capacity**: reduced China sales harm the companies whose technological edge makes the controls possible
- **Small items defeat controls**: chips are produced by millions, small, easily concealed; design software can cross borders undetected
- **The controls may be self-defeating over 10-20 years** if China achieves self-sufficiency while US firms lose competitive edge

**Game mechanism:** Technology denial has diminishing returns over time. Short-term disruption vs. long-term acceleration of rival capabilities. The enforcer pays economic costs that compound over decades.

### Alliance Fragmentation

From NATO's experience:
- **Disappearance of the original threat reduces cohesion.** After the Soviet collapse, the penalty for disagreements dropped and alternative paths for national interests multiplied.
- **Burden-sharing fights are chronic.** In Libya (2011), only 8 of 28 NATO members participated in combat. The 2% GDP target has been a persistent source of friction.
- **Different threat perceptions drive different priorities.** Front-line states (Baltics, Poland) care about Russia; southern states care about migration and instability. These competing priorities fragment consensus.
- **After Ukraine invasion (2022): mixed results.** Initial unity on sanctions, but disagreements emerged over how much aid, when to negotiate, and Ukraine's membership path.

**Game mechanism:** Alliances hold when the threat is visible and shared. As the original crisis fades from memory, allies develop divergent threat perceptions and competing economic interests. The coalition slowly fragments unless periodically shocked back into alignment.

## 3. The Role of Crises and Shocks

### Do Crises Strengthen or Weaken Enforcement?

Both — the direction depends on crisis type and regime design:

**Crises that STRENGTHEN enforcement:**
- **Disasters that validate the threat model.** Chernobyl transformed nuclear safety culture globally and contributed to the Soviet Union's political openings. Fukushima triggered Germany's nuclear exit and Japan's safety reforms.
- **Near-misses that make the threat vivid.** After the Cuban Missile Crisis, both superpowers moved toward arms control (Partial Test Ban Treaty 1963, NPT 1968).
- **Violations that are publicly exposed.** Syria's chemical weapons use ultimately led to stronger OPCW investigation powers (though this took years of political fighting).

**Crises that WEAKEN enforcement:**
- **Economic crises that make compliance costly.** The 1873 financial crisis led countries to abandon trade agreements. States facing economic pressure seek "temporary relief from obligations" — which often becomes permanent.
- **Wars that realign great power relationships.** The Franco-Prussian War prompted widespread abandonment of tariff reduction agreements. Ukraine conflict led Russia to withdraw its CTBT ratification.
- **Crises that consume political attention.** Pandemics, financial crashes, and regional wars distract from enforcement. The enforcement agency loses salience.

**Key asymmetry:** Crises related to the treaty's subject matter tend to strengthen it (nuclear accidents strengthen nuclear safety). Unrelated crises tend to weaken it by consuming political capital and creating economic pressure to defect.

### Nuclear Accidents and Public Opinion: Non-Obvious Dynamics

- After Chernobyl, confidence in nuclear safety actually *increased* in countries not directly affected — the visible consequences elsewhere made people feel their own systems were safer by comparison.
- After Fukushima, public support for nuclear energy didn't drop immediately. It remained high until 2014 (three years later), then declined steadily through 2021. **The effect is delayed, not instant.**
- Japan's nuclear industry had systematically avoided absorbing lessons from Three Mile Island and Chernobyl, covering up small incidents. **Industries capture their regulators over time.**
- Independent oversight matters more than any other factor for safety culture. Regulatory capture is the primary failure mode.

**Game mechanism:** Disasters don't just change policy — they have delayed, non-linear effects on public opinion. Regulatory capture is a slow process that makes catastrophe more likely. Near-misses can go either way: they sometimes prevent disaster (wake-up call) and sometimes create complacency ("we survived that, so the risk is manageable").

## 4. Information Environment Evolution

### OSINT and Treaty Verification

The information environment has been transformed over 30 years:

- **Commercial satellite imagery** (Planet Labs, Maxar) allows anyone to monitor military and industrial sites. Bellingcat geolocated missile strikes in Ukraine using social media and satellite imagery.
- **Social media monitoring** enables pattern detection: workforce movements, construction activity, supply chain anomalies.
- **The verification workflow** has been democratized: persistent satellite monitoring → event detection → social media cross-referencing → pattern analysis. This used to require state intelligence agencies; now NGOs and journalists do it.

**Game mechanism:** OSINT progressively makes secret programs harder to hide, but it also creates information overload. The challenge shifts from "can we detect violations?" to "can we distinguish real violations from noise/deception?"

### Deepfakes and Disinformation as Weapons Against Enforcement

- **The "Liar's Dividend":** Once deepfakes exist, authentic evidence becomes deniable. States accused of violations can claim evidence is fabricated. This undermines the credibility of inspection findings and whistleblower reports.
- **Informational trust decay:** Public trust in authorities and official facts erodes when realistic fakes proliferate. Treaty verification depends on the credibility of the verifying institution.
- **Fragmented governance:** Media law, cybersecurity policy, and AI governance operate in silos, creating exploitable gaps.
- **Narrative warfare:** States use propaganda to delegitimize enforcement institutions. China promotes "smart city" surveillance technology globally while undermining Western-led governance frameworks. Russia uses information operations to fracture Western consensus.

**Game mechanism:** Over 30 years, the information environment shifts from "evidence is hard to get" to "evidence is easy to get but impossible to make credible." Adversaries weaponize the information environment against the enforcement agency's legitimacy.

## 5. Key Dynamics for a ~30-Year Timeframe

### What Changes on Decadal Timescales?

**Decade 1 (Years 1-10): Institutional Building**
- Treaty institutions are new and have founding-generation political support
- Verification systems are being built and tested
- Technology denial is most effective (gap between leaders and followers is large)
- Public memory of the founding crisis is fresh
- Insincere members are positioning themselves but haven't been tested yet
- Economic costs of compliance are lowest (alternatives haven't been foreclosed)

**Decade 2 (Years 11-20): Stress Testing**
- Original political leaders retire; successors lack personal commitment to the treaty
- Technology diffusion narrows the gap despite controls (A.Q. Khan-style networks emerge)
- First serious compliance crises test the regime (Syria-type situations)
- Economic costs of compliance rise as restricted technology becomes more commercially valuable
- Alliance fatigue sets in; burden-sharing fights intensify
- OSINT capabilities improve, making violations harder to hide but also enabling "liar's dividend" defenses
- The treaty either adapts (Montreal Protocol model: amendments, new protocols) or enters zombie state (NPT model: institution persists, consensus dies)

**Decade 3 (Years 21-30): Regime Transformation or Collapse**
- Generational turnover is nearly complete; younger cohorts have different threat perceptions
- Technology has diffused broadly; restricted capabilities are becoming commoditized
- Rising powers demand governance reform reflecting new power realities
- The original technology control chokepoints may no longer exist (domestic substitutes developed)
- De-dollarization and shifting economic power change who has leverage over whom
- The treaty either has adapted enough to remain relevant or is a hollow shell

### Generational Turnover in Leadership and Public Memory

Research findings:
- Each generation's foreign policy attitudes are shaped by the era they came of age in. Younger Americans are less hawkish, less supportive of military interventions, and less committed to alliances than older generations.
- Leadership turnover makes treaty ratification less likely when new leaders rely on different domestic constituencies.
- **The foreign policy consensus has a "rapidly approaching expiration date"** — generational replacement is slow but inexorable.
- In non-democracies, leadership change can produce dramatic foreign policy shifts (new leader, new support base, new priorities).

**Game mechanism:** Every 8-12 years, a generational shift in key countries' leadership changes the political calculus. Leaders who lived through the founding crisis retire; their successors see the treaty as inherited constraint rather than hard-won achievement. Public memory of *why* the treaty exists fades.

### Technology Diffusion Making Restricted Capabilities Widely Available

The "democratization of dual-use technology" pattern:
- What was cutting-edge 15 years ago becomes widely available (AI, CNC machining, 3D printing, robotics)
- Diffusion pathways: buy, beg, steal, or copy. Export controls can block buying but not the other three.
- **The paradox:** Restricting access accelerates indigenous development. US chip controls pushed China toward semiconductor self-sufficiency.
- Small, concealable items defeat export controls. Large, specialized equipment is the only reliable chokepoint.
- Non-state actors gain access to capabilities that were state-monopoly a decade earlier.

**Game mechanism:** The difficulty of enforcement ratchets up every decade as the underlying technology becomes easier to acquire independently. The game should feel progressively harder.

### Shifting Economic Power and Leverage

- BRICS nations now represent >25% of global GDP and ~50% of world population
- De-dollarization efforts (BRICS pay systems, local-currency lending via NDB) gradually erode Western financial leverage
- But dollar still dominates (~59% of global reserves vs yuan's <5%) — economic leverage shifts slowly
- Rising powers want governance reform (voting shares in IMF/World Bank, UN Security Council seats)
- They maintain strategic autonomy: India works with the US in the Quad while partnering with China/Russia in BRICS
- **The leverage tool kit changes:** Financial sanctions become less effective as alternative payment systems develop; economic interdependence cuts both ways

**Game mechanism:** Over 30 years, the enforcement agency's economic leverage slowly erodes. Sanctions that worked in Year 5 are circumvented by Year 20. New coalitions form around alternative financial infrastructure. The agency must find non-economic sources of leverage as economic tools weaken.

## 6. Repeating Patterns for the Game

### Pattern 1: The Compliance Spiral
Good-faith members see insincere members getting away with violations → good-faith members reduce their own compliance → the regime norm erodes → more violations. This is the single most common treaty failure mode.

### Pattern 2: The Adaptation Race
Technology evolves → existing verification methods become obsolete → the treaty institution must develop new methods → political resistance to expanding inspection powers → a window of vulnerability before new methods are adopted. Montreal Protocol won this race; the CWC is losing it.

### Pattern 3: The Alliance Maintenance Tax
Keeping the coalition together requires constant side-payments, reassurance, and threat-refreshing. As the original threat fades, the cost of alliance maintenance rises while the perceived benefit falls.

### Pattern 4: The Technological Hourglass
Early on, technology is concentrated and controllable (few suppliers, large equipment, specialized knowledge). Over time, knowledge diffuses, manufacturing becomes distributed, and alternatives emerge. Enforcement difficulty ratchets up monotonically. The game should get harder every decade.

### Pattern 5: The Crisis Paradox
The enforcement agency needs crises to maintain political support (nothing refreshes treaty commitment like a near-miss). But crises also create opportunities for defection (economic pressure, distraction, realignment). The agency must be lucky: it needs the *right kind* of crisis at the *right time*.

### Pattern 6: The Institutional Capture Cycle
Enforcement agencies are gradually captured by the interests they regulate. Industry representation on advisory panels → watered-down standards → regulatory complacency → catastrophic failure → reform → new cycle begins.

### Pattern 7: The Generational Forgetting
Founding generation retires → institutional memory weakens → new leaders treat the treaty as bureaucratic overhead rather than existential necessity → enforcement atrophies → the original risk reasserts itself.
