# Concept Evaluation: WATCHPOINT vs Treaty Builder

## Verdict

**Treaty Builder is the stronger concept for this project's goals.** It wins on the two links that matter most: Learn (articulable knowledge production) and Complete (dropout resistance through the mid-experience). WATCHPOINT is a better *game* but a worse *teaching tool* for the specific kind of knowledge this project needs to produce — talking points a player can repeat to a friend.

The core asymmetry: WATCHPOINT teaches enforcement dynamics through spatial intuition and felt experience. Treaty Builder teaches pause requirements through explicit propositions that the player evaluates, debates internally, and can later articulate. The project needs articulable knowledge. WATCHPOINT produces felt understanding. Treaty Builder produces articulable knowledge by construction — every provision the player adds is a sentence they read, evaluated, and approved.

This verdict comes with a significant caveat: Treaty Builder's bottleneck is Encounter (it sounds boring in a tweet). WATCHPOINT's bottleneck is Learn (it teaches the wrong *kind* of knowledge). Encounter is addressable through marketing, naming, and framing. Learn is structural. You can rename a game; you cannot make spatial intuition articulable after the fact.

## Link-by-Link Analysis

### 1. ENCOUNTER (awareness through the click/open decision)

The question: does someone who sees a link/description click it?

#### Sub-factors

| Factor | WATCHPOINT | Treaty Builder | Edge |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Concept hook** (can you explain it in one sentence?) | "Place surveillance tools on a supply chain and catch rogue AI labs before they build superintelligence" — clear, visual, exciting | "Add provisions to an AI treaty and watch them fail" — abstract, sounds bureaucratic | WATCHPOINT |
| **Genre recognition** (does it pattern-match to something the audience already likes?) | Matches "tower defense / Papers Please / monitoring sim" — recognized genres | No obvious genre match. Closest: "quiz" or "choose your own adventure" — weaker hooks | WATCHPOINT |
| **Thumbnail/preview potential** (what does a screenshot look like?) | A schematic with nodes, tools, pulsing signals — visually interesting, looks like a game | A scrollable list with colored rows — looks like a settings page or a form | WATCHPOINT |
| **Share-bait title** (would you click this on Twitter?) | "WATCHPOINT" sounds like a game. Spy/surveillance connotation is intriguing | "Treaty Builder" sounds like homework. Even with a better name, the underlying concept is hard to pitch as fun | WATCHPOINT |
| **Target audience match** (people who don't yet appreciate how a pause works) | "Come play this surveillance game" might attract people who wouldn't read a policy paper | "Come build a treaty" attracts people already interested in governance — preaching to choir | WATCHPOINT |

**Assessment:** WATCHPOINT wins Encounter clearly. It sounds like a game. Treaty Builder sounds like an educational exercise. A potential player scrolling Twitter would click WATCHPOINT's link; they'd scroll past Treaty Builder's.

**Confidence:** High. This is the most evaluable link — we can directly compare how each concept would look in a tweet, a link preview, a screenshot. WATCHPOINT looks like a game; Treaty Builder looks like a form.

**What would close the gap:** Treaty Builder could be reframed. "Can you build a treaty that survives 30 years?" is more compelling than "add provisions to a treaty." A provocative framing — "99% of players fail by year 15" — helps. A good name helps. But the underlying visual (a list) is hard to make exciting in a thumbnail.

### 2. COMPLETE (everything from URL open through finishing the experience)

This is the highest-variance link. Modeled at six exit points:

#### 2a. Bounce (first 10 seconds)

The player has loaded the page. They see the initial screen. Do they interact or close the tab?

| Factor | WATCHPOINT | Treaty Builder |
|---|---|---|
| **Visual first impression** | A schematic with nodes and edges. Animated chip flow. Looks like something is happening. But: on a 390px screen, a graph with labels may look cluttered or confusing. "What am I looking at?" | A single failure-mode card at the top, a few proposed provisions below. Clean, readable. The player immediately sees text they can read and understand. |
| **Cognitive load** | High. The player sees a diagram they don't understand, icons they can't interpret, and labels (FACTORY, SHIPPING, DATA CENTER) that require parsing. Even session 1's 3-node schematic is a novel visual grammar. | Low. The player sees a failure description and some response options. The UX pattern (read problem, pick solution) is universally familiar. |
| **"What do I do?"** | Unclear until the tutorial prompt appears. The player's first instinct may be to tap things — but which things? Where? The spatial grammar has to be learned. | Immediately clear. Read the failure, pick a provision. The interaction pattern is self-evident. |

**Assessment:** Treaty Builder has lower bounce risk. WATCHPOINT's schematic is visually interesting but cognitively demanding. Treaty Builder's list is visually plain but immediately comprehensible. On mobile, where attention is scarce and patience is low, comprehensibility wins over visual interest. Players bounce from confusion, not from boredom.

**Confidence:** Medium-high. Bounce behavior is well-studied: cognitive load and "what do I do?" confusion are the primary drivers. WATCHPOINT's novel visual grammar is a real bounce risk on first visit.

#### 2b. First interaction (first 30 seconds)

The player has survived the first impression. They attempt their first action. Does it feel satisfying?

| Factor | WATCHPOINT | Treaty Builder |
|---|---|---|
| **First action** | Tap a node or edge, then tap a tool from the palette. Two-step action with spatial reasoning. | Read a failure description, tap a provision. One-step action with reading comprehension. |
| **Feedback** | Tool appears on schematic, signals begin appearing. Visual, spatial, animated. Satisfying if the player understood what they did. Confusing if they placed the tool on the wrong target. | Provision moves to the "passed" section, failure resolves, next failure appears. Clear cause-and-effect. The reward is seeing the problem disappear. |
| **Failure cost** | Placing a tool on the wrong node wastes a tool slot. The player may not realize the mistake until later. Silent failure. | Picking a bad provision has visible consequences (the failure isn't resolved, or a new one appears). Immediate, legible feedback. |
| **Scaffolding needed** | Tutorial overlay required. Session 1 is a guided walkthrough precisely because the interaction is not self-evident. | Minimal scaffolding. "This failure threatens the pause. Which provision addresses it?" is self-explanatory. |

**Assessment:** Treaty Builder's first interaction is more reliable. WATCHPOINT's first interaction is more rewarding *when it works* but riskier — a confused first tap produces no clear feedback, and the player doesn't know they've failed. Treaty Builder's first interaction always produces legible feedback.

**Confidence:** Medium. First-interaction satisfaction is hard to predict without a prototype. WATCHPOINT's tutorial could be excellent. But tutorial-dependent first interactions are structurally weaker than self-evident ones.

#### 2c. Pattern recognition (first 2 minutes)

The player grasps the pattern. Does "I get it" convert to "I want more" or "more of the same"?

| Factor | WATCHPOINT | Treaty Builder |
|---|---|---|
| **Pattern** | "Place tools, read signals, catch threats, die, try again." | "See failure, add provision, failure resolved, next failure." |
| **Variety promise** | High. The schematic changes shape. New tools unlock. Signal types vary. The pattern is "same loop, different puzzle each time." Reigns-like: pattern recognition doesn't exhaust the content. | Medium. The loop is the same each time — read, pick, advance. Variety comes from *what* the failures and provisions are, not from how you interact with them. The pattern is a quiz. |
| **"I want more" driver** | Curiosity about the system. "What other tools exist? What happens when I cover this route?" The schematic invites exploration. | Curiosity about the content. "What failure comes next? Was I right about that provision?" This is page-turner curiosity, not exploration curiosity. |
| **"More of the same" risk** | Low in early sessions (new tools, new nodes). Higher after session 3-4 when the palette is full. | Higher. Once the player recognizes the pattern (read failure, pick answer), the question is whether the content is interesting enough to sustain attention. If the failures feel like a quiz, dropout spikes. |
| **Mastery hooks** | Strong. "I should have placed the satellite there, not here" — the player sees their mistakes spatially and wants to correct them. Reigns-like "I'll do better next time." | Weak for mastery, strong for completion. The player doesn't get "better" at picking provisions — they either know the answer or they guess. But "what's the next failure?" pulls them forward. |

**Assessment:** WATCHPOINT wins on replayability and mastery motivation. Treaty Builder wins on forward momentum within a single session. For a project that needs the teaching to land in the *first* session (most players won't return), Treaty Builder's forward momentum matters more than WATCHPOINT's replay loop.

**Confidence:** Medium. This depends heavily on content quality. A Treaty Builder with brilliant, surprising failure modes sustains attention through content alone. A Treaty Builder with predictable failures dies here. WATCHPOINT's system variety is more robust to content quality — the spatial puzzle provides engagement even if specific signals are mediocre.

**Unresolvable without prototype:** Whether "page-turner curiosity" or "exploration curiosity" produces longer sessions for this specific audience.

#### 2d. Mid-experience fatigue (2-5 minutes)

The player has been playing for a few minutes. Are they still engaged?

| Factor | WATCHPOINT | Treaty Builder |
|---|---|---|
| **Cognitive fatigue** | The schematic grows more complex. More signals to read, more tools to manage. Cognitive load increases steadily. Players who don't enjoy spatial puzzles hit a wall. | Reading load stays constant — each failure/provision pair is a self-contained unit. No accumulating complexity. Players pace themselves by scrolling. |
| **Decision fatigue** | Accumulates. Each turn adds more nodes, more threats, more signals to process. The frantic feeling is intentional but causes exits. | Low. Each decision is independent. The player never manages multiple simultaneous concerns. |
| **Progress legibility** | Safety bar advances slowly. "Am I winning?" is unclear for most of the session. | Highly legible. Passed provisions accumulate visibly. The scrollable list IS the progress indicator — "I've addressed 8 of N failures." |
| **Mobile-specific fatigue** | Spatial interaction on a small screen is tiring. Tapping 44px nodes while reading text is demanding. | Scrolling and tapping are low-effort mobile interactions. No precision targeting. |

**Assessment:** Treaty Builder has significantly lower mid-experience fatigue. WATCHPOINT's rising complexity is its teaching feature (enforcement gets harder) but also its dropout driver. The frantic feeling that teaches "the pause is not relaxed waiting" is the same feeling that makes players close the tab. Treaty Builder's constant-load design means the experience can be longer without fatigue, and the player can pause and resume more easily.

**Confidence:** High. Cognitive and decision fatigue are well-studied drivers of mobile game dropout. WATCHPOINT's accumulating complexity is a structural fatigue generator. Treaty Builder's independent-unit design is structurally fatigue-resistant.

#### 2e. Resolution satisfaction (end)

The player reaches an ending. Do they feel satisfied?

| Factor | WATCHPOINT | Treaty Builder |
|---|---|---|
| **Death experience** | The schematic freezes, highlighting the failure path in red. The player sees exactly where coverage failed. This is WATCHPOINT's strongest moment — visual, specific, non-preachy, and teaches a concrete lesson. | The treaty collapses when a failure overwhelms the provisions. The player sees which provisions were insufficient and why. This is a checklist review — less dramatic but more educational. |
| **"I learned something" feeling** | Strong for "I need to cover that route next time." Weak for "I understand why enforcement fails at scale." The lesson is tactical, not strategic. | Strong for "these specific things are needed for a pause to work." Each provision is a talking point. The player exits with a mental list of requirements. |
| **Restart motivation** | High. "I'll place my tools differently." Clear action hypothesis for next run. Reigns-like loop. | Low-medium. "I'll pick different provisions" is less compelling because the provisions don't interact spatially — there's no "where" to place them differently. |

**Assessment:** WATCHPOINT has a more satisfying death moment. Treaty Builder has a more educational one. For a project prioritizing teaching over retention, Treaty Builder's educational ending is more valuable — but WATCHPOINT's restart motivation matters for the Retain link (see below).

**Confidence:** Medium. Resolution satisfaction is hard to predict. WATCHPOINT's visual death screen is genuinely strong design. Treaty Builder's ending needs careful design to avoid feeling like a test result.

#### 2f. Interruption recovery (mobile-specific)

The player is interrupted (notification, phone call, context switch). Do they return?

| Factor | WATCHPOINT | Treaty Builder |
|---|---|---|
| **State complexity** | High. The player was managing a schematic with 6 nodes, 4 tools, active signals. Returning after an interruption means re-parsing the entire board state. | Low. The player was reading a failure description. They can resume from where they left off by reading the current failure again. |
| **Re-engagement cost** | High. "Where was I? What was that signal? Which tool was degraded?" The spatial state must be reconstructed from memory. | Low. "What failure am I looking at? Oh right — transshipment. What provisions address it?" Immediate re-engagement. |

**Assessment:** Treaty Builder wins on interruption recovery. This matters because mobile sessions are frequently interrupted. A player who can't easily re-engage after an interruption is a player who closes the tab.

**Confidence:** High. Interruption recovery correlates directly with state complexity. This is well-established in mobile UX research.

#### Complete: Overall Assessment

| Exit point | WATCHPOINT | Treaty Builder | Edge |
|---|---|---|---|
| Bounce (10s) | Higher risk (novel UI) | Lower risk (familiar pattern) | Treaty Builder |
| First interaction (30s) | Riskier but more rewarding | Safer, always legible | Treaty Builder |
| Pattern recognition (2min) | Stronger mastery hook | Stronger forward momentum | Draw (depends on audience) |
| Mid-experience fatigue (2-5min) | Accumulating cognitive load | Constant, manageable load | Treaty Builder |
| Resolution (end) | More satisfying death | More educational death | Draw (different values) |
| Interruption recovery | Hard to resume | Easy to resume | Treaty Builder |

**Treaty Builder wins Complete.** It has lower dropout risk at four of six exit points. WATCHPOINT's advantages (mastery hook, satisfying death) are real but matter less for a project where most players will play once.

**Confidence:** Medium-high overall. The individual assessments range from medium to high confidence. The overall pattern is consistent: Treaty Builder's simpler interaction model produces lower friction at every exit point except pattern recognition and resolution.

### 3. LEARN (player develops the correct mental model)

The teaching goal is talking points about what a pause requires. Three types of knowledge to evaluate:

- **Articulable knowledge** — the player can explain it to a friend. "A pause needs X because Y."
- **Felt understanding** — the player has intuition but can't articulate it. "Enforcement is hard, trust me."
- **Factual recall** — the player remembers specific facts. "ASML makes lithography machines."

The project needs articulable knowledge. Factual recall is useful only if it supports articulable knowledge. Felt understanding is valuable but insufficient — a player who "feels" that enforcement is hard but can't explain why is a player who can't transmit the knowledge.

#### WATCHPOINT's knowledge production

**What the player learns interactively (through doing):**
- Coverage gaps get exploited (felt understanding: strong)
- You can't monitor everything (felt understanding: strong)
- Tools become obsolete against specific threats (felt understanding: moderate)
- Maintenance competes with operations (felt understanding: moderate)

**What the player learns textually (through reading signals/events):**
- What each monitoring tool does (factual recall)
- What evasion methods exist (factual recall)
- Why inspectors get captured (articulable if the player reads and remembers the audit text)
- Why algorithmic progress matters (articulable if the player reads the explanation)
- Political constraints on enforcement (factual recall of specific events)

**What the player does NOT learn:**
- Why a pause is necessary in the first place (assumed context)
- What safety research involves (safety bar advances but the player doesn't know what drives it)
- Why institutional decay happens systemically (individual cases are taught; the pattern is not)
- M16 (willful ignorance) and M12 (conflicting mandates) — no interaction path
- The connection between enforcement and the broader pause logic

**Knowledge type produced:** Primarily felt understanding (spatial intuition about coverage, tool limits, escalating difficulty) with some factual recall (tool names, evasion types). Articulable knowledge is produced only for players who carefully read text readouts AND retain them, which is a small fraction of mobile players.

**Articulable talking points a WATCHPOINT player could deliver to a friend:**
1. "There are only a few chip factories in the world, so you can monitor them" (maybe — depends on reading the ASML text)
2. "People find ways around the monitoring" (vague felt understanding, not specific)
3. "The enforcement tools stop working over time" (vague felt understanding)
4. "It's really hard to catch everyone" (vague felt understanding)

These are weak talking points. They communicate "enforcement is hard" (which people already vaguely believe) but not "enforcement requires these specific things" (which is what the project needs to teach).

#### Treaty Builder's knowledge production

**What the player learns interactively (through choosing provisions):**
- Each failure mode exists and is named (factual recall: strong)
- Each provision addresses a specific failure mode (articulable knowledge: strong by construction)
- Some provisions that sound good don't actually work (articulable knowledge: strong if the misleading provisions are well-designed)
- More provisions are needed than expected (felt understanding of scope)
- Failures compound — addressing one reveals another (felt understanding of system complexity)

**What the player learns textually (through reading failures/provisions):**
- Specific failure scenarios: distributed training, shell companies, institutional capture (factual recall + articulable knowledge)
- Specific enforcement measures: chip tracking, financial monitoring, inspector protocols (factual recall + articulable knowledge)
- Why each measure is necessary — the failure it prevents (articulable knowledge by construction: the player just read the failure and picked the provision)

**What the player does NOT learn:**
- The spatial/operational feel of enforcement (no spatial intuition)
- The frantic pace of real-time threat management (no time pressure)
- The emotional weight of making wrong calls under uncertainty (no consequence uncertainty)
- How enforcement degrades gradually rather than failing discretely (provisions either work or don't)

**Knowledge type produced:** Primarily articulable knowledge (each provision is a sentence the player evaluated and approved) with strong factual recall (failure modes are named and described) and moderate felt understanding (the accumulating list of necessary provisions produces a "this is more than I expected" feeling).

**Articulable talking points a Treaty Builder player could deliver to a friend:**
1. "You need to track every AI chip from factory to deployment because otherwise people build hidden clusters" (specific, articulable)
2. "Inspectors get captured by the companies they regulate — you need independent audits" (specific, articulable)
3. "Countries can design their monitoring to be just bad enough to miss violations on purpose" (specific, articulable, M16 taught explicitly)
4. "The agency that enforces the pause gets funded by the same countries it's supposed to police" (specific, articulable, M12 taught explicitly)
5. "Shell companies can buy chips through legitimate channels — you need financial monitoring too" (specific, articulable)
6. "Algorithmic progress means the hardware threshold keeps dropping, so monitoring that worked five years ago doesn't work anymore" (specific, articulable)

These are strong talking points. Each one names a specific problem and its solution. A friend hearing these can repeat them. This is the knowledge type the project needs.

#### Learn: Overall Assessment

| Knowledge type | WATCHPOINT | Treaty Builder | Project need |
|---|---|---|---|
| Articulable knowledge | Weak — produced only through text readouts, which most players skim | Strong — produced by construction (each provision is a sentence) | **Primary need** |
| Felt understanding | Strong — spatial intuition, frantic pace, "enforcement is hard" | Moderate — "more provisions than expected," no spatial feel | Secondary |
| Factual recall | Moderate — tool names, evasion types if player reads text | Strong — failure modes and provisions are explicitly named | Supports articulable knowledge |

**Treaty Builder wins Learn decisively.** It produces articulable knowledge by construction. Every provision the player adds to the treaty is a talking point they can repeat. WATCHPOINT produces felt understanding — which is valuable but cannot be transmitted verbally. A player who exits WATCHPOINT thinking "enforcement is really hard" cannot explain *why* to a friend in specific terms. A player who exits Treaty Builder thinking "you need chip tracking, independent audits, financial monitoring, and algorithmic threshold adjustments" can explain each one.

**Confidence:** High. The distinction between felt understanding and articulable knowledge is well-established in educational psychology. Spatial/interactive experiences produce felt understanding (you can DO it but can't EXPLAIN it — like riding a bicycle). Propositional/evaluative experiences produce articulable knowledge (you can EXPLAIN it because you evaluated each proposition). The project needs articulable knowledge. Treaty Builder produces it structurally.

**The strongest argument against this assessment:** A WATCHPOINT player who replays many sessions and reads the text readouts carefully accumulates articulable knowledge. The multi-session progression builds vocabulary over time. This is true but requires 5+ sessions of engaged play with careful text reading — a behavior pattern that describes a tiny fraction of mobile web visitors. Treaty Builder produces articulable knowledge in a single session because the interaction IS propositional evaluation.

### 4. RETAIN (player remembers the talking points hours/days later)

The question: does the knowledge persist?

#### Sub-factors

| Factor | WATCHPOINT | Treaty Builder |
|---|---|---|
| **Encoding strength** | Strong for spatial memories (the schematic, the failure path in red). Weak for text content (signal readouts are read once and forgotten). | Strong for propositions that were actively evaluated. The player didn't passively read each provision — they decided whether it addressed the failure. Active evaluation produces stronger encoding than passive reading. |
| **Retrieval cues** | "That game where I placed surveillance tools on a map" — the spatial image is memorable but doesn't cue specific talking points. | "That game where I had to figure out what the treaty needed" — the failure-provision pairs are the retrieval units. Each failure is a story that cues its provision. |
| **Repetition** | Multi-session play repeats spatial patterns (coverage gaps, tool placement). Repetition strengthens felt understanding but doesn't repeat specific propositions. | Within a single session, the player encounters multiple failure-provision pairs. If the game is well-designed, some provisions recur (they fail the first time and must be strengthened or replaced). Cross-session repetition if the player replays. |
| **Distinctiveness** | The schematic and surveillance metaphor are distinctive — players won't confuse this with other games. But the distinctiveness attaches to the EXPERIENCE, not to specific facts. | The failure scenarios are distinctive if well-written (M16 willful ignorance is a genuinely surprising concept for most people). The distinctiveness attaches to SPECIFIC PROPOSITIONS, which is what needs to be retained. |
| **Emotional encoding** | The "frantic effort" feeling and the frustration of dying from coverage gaps create emotional memories. Emotion strengthens retention — but of the experience, not of specific facts. | The "I was wrong about that provision" moment creates surprise-based encoding. Surprise strengthens retention of the specific proposition that surprised the player. |

**Assessment:** Treaty Builder produces more retainable articulable knowledge. WATCHPOINT produces more retainable experiential memory. For the project's goal (talking points retained for later transmission), Treaty Builder's proposition-level retention is more valuable.

**Confidence:** Medium. Retention is hard to predict without empirical testing. The theoretical advantage (active evaluation > passive reading for propositional retention) is supported by educational research, but the magnitude of the effect for this specific context is unknown.

**What would help WATCHPOINT:** If signal readouts were more prominent, repeated across sessions, and tested (the game quizzes the player on whether they remember what a specific tool detects), WATCHPOINT's text content would be more retainable. But this would make it more text-heavy, undermining its spatial advantage.

### 5. TRANSMIT (player shares or discusses with others)

The target reaction: "so good, need to share!"

#### Types of fun that drive sharing

| Fun type | WATCHPOINT | Treaty Builder |
|---|---|---|
| **Novelty fun** ("I've never seen anything like this") | Strong. A surveillance game where you place tools on a supply chain schematic is genuinely novel. "You have to play this weird spy game about AI chips." | Weak-moderate. A treaty-building exercise is not novel. The content might be surprising, but the format is familiar (quiz/checklist). |
| **Mastery fun** ("I got good at this") | Strong. "I finally survived past session 5 by auditing my inspector early." Share-worthy achievement. | Weak. "I picked the right provisions" is not an achievement people brag about. |
| **Social fun** ("I want to discuss this with someone") | Moderate. "Did you know there are only a few chip factories?" — but the player may not remember specific facts to discuss (see Learn assessment). | Strong. "Did you know that the agency enforcing the pause gets funded by the same countries it polices? And inspectors get captured by the companies they regulate?" — these are conversation starters. Each provision is a discussion topic. |
| **Insight fun** ("I learned something surprising") | Moderate for first-time players (enforcement is harder than expected). Low for repeat players (the insight doesn't deepen much past session 3). | Strong if failure modes are genuinely surprising. M16 (willful ignorance), M13 (cultural capture), M4 (turnkey proliferation networks) are all concepts that surprise people who haven't studied arms control. Each one is a "wait, really?" moment. |
| **Challenge fun** ("Can YOU survive?") | Strong. "I dare you to keep the pause alive past year 2045." Challenge-based sharing is viral. | Weak. "I dare you to build a treaty" is not a compelling challenge. |

**Assessment:** Mixed. WATCHPOINT has stronger viral mechanics (challenge fun, novelty fun, mastery fun). Treaty Builder has stronger discussion-starting mechanics (social fun, insight fun). The question is which type of sharing this project needs.

For the project's goals, discussion-starting is more valuable than viral challenge-sharing. The goal is not "many people play the game" (that's Encounter, not Transmit). The goal is "a player who played the game transmits specific talking points to a friend." A player who shares WATCHPOINT says "play this game, it's cool." A player who shares Treaty Builder says "did you know that enforcement needs X, Y, and Z?" The first drives Encounter for new players. The second IS transmission of the target knowledge.

**However:** WATCHPOINT's challenge-sharing drives the full Encounter-Complete-Learn-Retain loop for new players, which may produce more total knowledge transmission through volume. Treaty Builder's discussion-sharing transmits knowledge directly without requiring the friend to play, but reaches fewer people.

**Net assessment:** Treaty Builder wins on *quality* of transmission (specific talking points shared directly). WATCHPOINT wins on *quantity* of transmission (more people encounter the game through challenge-sharing). Which matters more depends on how many people need to play vs. how accurately the knowledge needs to be transmitted. For a project that values accuracy of mental model over reach, Treaty Builder wins.

**Confidence:** Low-medium. Sharing behavior is notoriously hard to predict. The distinction between challenge-sharing and discussion-sharing is real but the relative magnitudes are unknown.

## Bottleneck Analysis

### WATCHPOINT's bottleneck: Learn

WATCHPOINT's weakest link is Learn. It produces the wrong *type* of knowledge for the project's goals. Felt understanding ("enforcement is really hard") does not convert to articulable talking points ("enforcement requires X because Y"). This is a structural problem, not an execution problem.

**Is it addressable?** Partially. More prominent text, post-session summaries, explicit vocabulary building could shift the balance toward articulable knowledge. But every such addition makes WATCHPOINT more text-heavy, eroding its core advantage (spatial interactive learning). The fundamental tension: making WATCHPOINT teach articulable knowledge requires making it less interactive, which was its reason for existing. You cannot add text to a spatial game until it produces articulable knowledge without making it a text game with a spatial wrapper.

**Structural or execution?** Structural. The spatial-interaction-to-felt-understanding pipeline is inherent to the medium. You would need to redesign the core loop to produce articulable knowledge — at which point it's a different concept.

### Treaty Builder's bottleneck: Encounter

Treaty Builder's weakest link is Encounter. It sounds boring. "Build a treaty" does not make people click. The visual (a scrollable list) does not make people share screenshots. There is no obvious viral hook.

**Is it addressable?** Yes, substantially. Naming, framing, and marketing can compensate:
- A provocative name (not "Treaty Builder" — something like "THE LAST SAFEGUARD" or "HOLD THE LINE")
- A challenge frame ("Can you build a treaty that survives 30 years?")
- A failure-forward pitch ("99% of players' treaties collapse by year 15")
- Visual design that looks more like a game (progress indicators, threat visualizations, a "years survived" counter)
- A compelling opening screen that shows stakes before showing the list

None of these require changing the core interaction. They're marketing and UX polish applied to a sound concept.

**Structural or execution?** Execution. The underlying concept (evaluate failure modes, build a robust treaty) is sound. The presentation can be made compelling. This is solvable with design effort, not architectural redesign.

### Comparative bottleneck severity

WATCHPOINT has a structural bottleneck (wrong knowledge type) that cannot be fully addressed without redesigning the core loop. Treaty Builder has an execution bottleneck (boring first impression) that can be addressed with naming, framing, and visual design. Execution problems are solvable; structural problems are not.

## Secondary Considerations

### Implementation cost

| | WATCHPOINT | Treaty Builder |
|---|---|---|
| Estimated build time | 6-8 weeks (per the WATCHPOINT doc, which appears honest) | 2-3 weeks |
| Technical risk | Graph rendering, tool placement, signal generation, decay simulation — multiple novel systems | Scrollable list, conditional content display, state tracking — all standard patterns |

Treaty Builder is substantially cheaper to build, lower-risk technically, and easier to iterate on. For a project with limited development resources (one engineer-equivalent via agents), this matters.

### Alignment with core thesis

The core thesis has four components. How each concept serves them:

| Thesis component | WATCHPOINT | Treaty Builder |
|---|---|---|
| "Without a serious pause we are dead" | Assumed, not taught | Can be taught through the opening frame: "Without these measures, an unauthorized training run succeeds within 5 years" |
| "A serious pause is possible" | Implied by the game's existence (you can play the enforcer) | Directly taught: the player builds a working treaty, proving it's possible |
| "It requires specific things" | Taught vaguely (tool types, coverage) | Taught explicitly (each provision is a specific requirement) |
| "Talking points retained through repetition" | Repetition through replay sessions (requires return visits) | Repetition within a single session (multiple failure-provision pairs reinforce each other) + cross-session replay |

Treaty Builder aligns better with components 2, 3, and 4. WATCHPOINT is neutral on all four — it teaches the *feel* of enforcement but not the *substance* of the thesis.

## What This Evaluation Cannot Determine

1. **Whether Treaty Builder is actually fun.** The analysis argues it's educationally superior. But if it's boring, nobody completes it, and all the educational advantages are moot. Treaty Builder's fun depends entirely on content quality — the failure modes must be surprising, the misleading provisions must be genuinely tempting, and the "what goes wrong next?" pull must be strong. This cannot be evaluated without a prototype.

2. **Whether "page-turner curiosity" sustains a 5-minute mobile session.** Treaty Builder's engagement model is closer to a BuzzFeed quiz than a game. BuzzFeed quizzes work — but they're 90 seconds, not 5 minutes. Whether the model sustains longer attention depends on content pacing.

3. **Whether the scrollable list UX can be made visually engaging.** The current description (collapsible rows, color coding) could be excellent or could look like a settings panel. Visual design makes or breaks this.

4. **The relative value of WATCHPOINT's viral potential.** If WATCHPOINT generates 10x more players through challenge-sharing, the lower per-player knowledge production might be offset by volume. This is unknowable without deployment.

5. **Whether the target audience (people who don't yet appreciate pauses) would actually share either concept.** Both concepts assume the player is moved enough to share. The target audience may be too unfamiliar with the topic to feel compelled to share at all.
