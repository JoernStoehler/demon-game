# Sanctions by the Numbers: 2024 Year in Review

**Authors:** Eleanor Hume, Kyle Rutter
**Publisher:** Center for a New American Security (CNAS)
**Date:** March 11, 2025
**URL:** https://www.cnas.org/publications/reports/sanctions-by-the-numbers-2024-year-in-review

---

## Executive Summary

In 2024, the United States issued an unprecedented number of financial sanctions and entity-based export controls. Washington's economic statecraft has expanded in recent years, largely in response to Russia's war in Ukraine, competition with the People's Republic of China, and tensions in the Middle East. These geopolitical developments, along with escalating concerns about deepening ties among U.S. adversaries, shaped how the United States deployed its coercive economic toolkit last year.

**Key Statistics:**

- Treasury Department added 3,135 persons to the SDN List in 2024 (25% increase from 2023's 2,502)
- Commerce Department placed 520 persons on Entity List in 2024 (11% increase from 2023's 468)
- Russia received 1,706 SDN designations
- China received 276 SDN designations and 263 Entity List additions
- Russia received 147 Entity List additions

---

## SDN List Designations

The Treasury Department added 3,135 persons to the Specially Designated Nationals and Blocked Persons (SDN) List in 2024, targeting activities contrary to American national security and foreign policy interests.

### Country-Specific Financial Sanctions

**Russia (70% of 2024 designations):**

In 2024, 70 percent of SDN designations targeted Russia. The United States, often coordinating with allies and partners, issued sanctions targeting Russia's energy and finance sectors, military-industrial complex, chemical and biological weapons programs, and sanctions evasion networks.

**Third-Party Evasion and Support Networks:**

Despite past efforts to counter Russian evasion networks, Russia continued receiving critical military inputs, dual-use goods, and financial support from abroad. The Treasury Department's Office of Foreign Assets Control (OFAC) designated 70 non-Russian nationals and 459 entities with non-Russian addresses (including 14 with addresses both inside and outside Russia). These 529 persons spanned 55 third-party countries, with the highest number levied on Chinese persons.

Sanctions on non-Russian persons under Russia-related authorities grew significantly between 2022 and 2024. To crack down on evasion networks, Treasury broadened its authority to sanction foreign financial institutions supporting Russia's war effort. As of June 2024, any foreign financial institution conducting transactions on behalf of blocked persons under Executive Order 14024 became subject to sanctions. However, OFAC had not placed secondary sanctions on any foreign banks under this authority as of the report's publication.

**Energy and Finance:**

In 2024, the United States ratcheted up pressure on Russia's energy and finance sectors. The Treasury Department designated Gazprombank and its six foreign subsidiaries. Gazprombank is partially held by Kremlin-owned Gazprom and serves as a channel facilitating military-related financial activities and energy payments.

The administration also sanctioned persons contributing to future Russian energy capabilities, including Arctic Liquified Natural Gas 2, Vostok Oil, and Yakutia LNG projects.

During the final month of the Biden administration, Treasury took aggressive steps against Russia's energy sector (actions fall outside this report's analysis period). This included authorizing sanctions on any person operating in Russia's energy sector and sanctioning two major oil producers and over 180 vessels involved in illicit Russian oil trade. Treasury also tightened restrictions on energy-related transactions with certain sanctioned Russian banks and designated over 150 entities operating in Russia's defense, finance, and energy sectors or part of evasion networks.

**Iran (236 designations under Iran-specific authorities):**

The United States levied 236 designations targeting persons involved in illicit revenue-generating schemes, procuring military goods, and supplying equipment to Russian military forces. Designations also targeted persons supporting critical Iranian economic sectors including construction, mining, manufacturing, and textiles, and entities engaged in significant sales of Iranian petroleum and petrochemical products.

The U.S., often coordinating with Canada and Australia, sanctioned persons responsible for human rights violations and political repression in Iran, including members of the Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps (IRGC) and the Quds Force, which works with Iranian regional affiliates like Hezbollah and Hamas.

**North Korea (29 designations):**

The United States issued 29 designations reinforcing long-standing efforts to disrupt funding for the Kim regime's weapons of mass destruction program. Other sanctions targeted key Korean People's Army officials (including generals accompanying North Korean troops in Ukraine), Russian entities shipping petroleum to North Korea, and persons facilitating military assistance transfers between Russia and North Korea.

North Korea's ties with Russia became a significant concern in 2024 following a landmark mutual defense treaty and reports of North Korean soldiers deployed to support Russia in Ukraine.

**Other Country-Specific Authorities:**

The Biden administration used financial sanctions addressing Belarus (55 designations), Venezuela (37 designations), and the West Bank (33 designations). Belarus sanctions targeted support for Russia's invasion and revenue streams of oligarchs supporting the Lukashenko regime. Venezuela sanctions responded to President Maduro's fraudulent 2024 election victory. President Biden issued a new executive order authorizing sanctions against Israeli settlers responsible for destabilizing West Bank activities (the second Trump administration revoked this order).

### Thematic Financial Sanctions

Thematic sanctions authorities counter transnational activities undermining U.S. interests rather than targeting specific countries. The majority targeted persons facilitating terrorist acts, illegal drug production and trafficking, and WMD proliferation.

**Counterterrorism:**

Nearly half of all thematic sanctions in 2024 targeted counterterrorism, marking the third consecutive year as the most frequently used thematic authority. Iran accounted for most counterterrorism sanctions with 58 designations in 2024, compared to 16 in 2023.

Following Iran's April 13, 2024, attack on Israel, the United States sanctioned 17 Iranian persons supporting Iran's ballistic missile and UAV programs and proliferating UAVs to IRGC regional affiliates. The United Kingdom concurrently imposed sanctions.

China received the second-highest number of designations under counterterrorism authorities, underscoring concerns about Chinese entities—especially in Hong Kong—in illicit terrorist financing networks. The Yemen-based Houthis became increasingly frequent counterterrorism sanctions recipients as the group persisted attacking U.S. vessels in the Red Sea.

Sanctions aimed at Houthis targeted Yemeni persons and disrupted the transnational network of Houthi financier Sa'id al-Jamal. The Biden administration maintained focus on sanctioning Hamas networks, designating 12 Palestinian persons in January for transferring funds, including cryptocurrency, from IRGC to Hamas. The United Kingdom and Australia concurrently sanctioned Hamas officials. In March, the United States and United Kingdom coordinated sanctions against a London-based Hamas fundraising network.

**Drug Trafficking:**

The number of persons sanctioned for illicit drug involvement fell from 192 in 2023 to 138 in 2024. Mexico accounted for the overwhelming majority of counternarcotics sanctions over the past two years, with Sinaloa and Cartel Jalisco Nueva Generación cartels predominant as targets due to their central role in fentanyl and synthetic drug production and trafficking. Colombia accounted for the second-highest number of counternarcotics sanctions, predominantly targeting Clan del Golfo, one of the largest cocaine trafficking organizations.

The number of Chinese persons designated for illicit drug trade roles dropped sharply from 44 in 2023 to 2 in 2024, possibly resulting from an agreement between President Biden and Chinese Communist Party General Secretary Xi Jinping on resuming counternarcotics cooperation in November 2023, launching the U.S.-PRC Counternarcotics Working Group in January 2024.

On January 20, 2025, President Trump issued an executive order establishing a process for designating drug cartels as Foreign Terrorist Organizations or Specially Designated Global Terrorists. Members of Congress and past administrations considered such designations but decided against them due to concerns about potential ramifications on U.S.-Mexico relations and American businesses.

**Human Rights and Corruption:**

In 2024, 57 persons faced sanctions under authorities targeting human rights abuses and corruption, a significant decrease from 173 designations in 2021 when the first Summit for Democracy occurred.

Ahead of the third Summit for Democracy, the United States issued its first sanctions against actors proliferating commercial spyware under malign cyber activities authorities. These designations targeted the Intellexa Consortium, developing the Predator spyware. Predator and other commercial spyware were used by malign actors seeking U.S. data access and by authoritarian regimes surveilling citizens and targeting dissidents.

Human rights and corruption sanctions targeted persons affiliated with Houthi prison systems and individuals in Zimbabwe, Cambodia, Georgia, Guyana, Uzbekistan, Haiti, and Russia. In March 2024, President Biden signed an executive order revoking Zimbabwe-specific EOs, resulting in removal of 121 persons from the SDN List. Concurrently, 12 persons affiliated with the Zimbabwean government, including its president (previously sanctioned under revoked EOs), were redesignated under human rights and corruption authorities—seemingly addressing humanitarian impact concerns.

**Malicious Cyber Activities and Election Interference:**

The number of persons sanctioned for malign cyber activities nearly doubled between 2023 and 2024. Russia received most cyber-related designations targeting Russian ransomware attacks against Australian healthcare insurers and members of Russian ransomware, hacktivist, and cybercrime groups.

China received the second-highest number of designations for malicious cyber activities. These targeted persons responsible for cyberattacks on U.S. critical infrastructure providers and spreading malware costing the U.S. government billions. 2024 marked the first time since 2020 that the United States sanctioned malign Chinese cyber actors under Executive Orders 13694 and 13757.

Executive Order 13848 authorizes sanctions on activities undermining electoral processes, targeting those "directly or indirectly engaged in, sponsored, concealed, or otherwise been complicit in foreign interference" in U.S. elections. Ten persons affiliated with IRGC and Russian intelligence faced designation under EO 13848 for interfering in 2020 and 2024 presidential elections.

Toward late 2024, Chinese hackers compromised the Treasury Department, including OFAC. One of the Biden administration's final sanctions series targeted persons involved in this cyberattack.

## Entity List Additions

In 2024, the Commerce Department added 520 persons to the Entity List. The list remained central to efforts curbing goods and technology flow to Russian military-industrial complex contributors and PRC technological and military advancement supporters.

### China (263 designations)

Two hundred sixty-three Chinese persons were placed on the Entity List in 2024, accounting for slightly over half of all additions. Notably, yearly Chinese Entity List additions surpassed Russian additions for the first time since Ukraine's conflict outbreak.

The administration's Chinese additions predominantly countered PRC military modernization and deepening ties with Russia and Iran.

**Advanced Semiconductors:**

Roughly half of Chinese Entity List additions (132 entities) targeted contributions to China's advanced semiconductor industry. These additions built on years-long efforts slowing China's chip advances, including 2024 expansion of broader technology-based semiconductor-related export controls introduced in 2022 and 2023. The Biden administration's efforts culminated in releasing the novel AI diffusion rule on January 13, 2025.

**Other Military Modernization:**

The Commerce Department added 55 persons for contributions to other PRC military modernization aspects, including at least 22 Chinese persons bolstering quantum capabilities and 11 involved in the high-altitude balloon program. Other additions targeted UAV program contributors, Chinese military pilot trainers, and those procuring U.S.-origin items and technologies for Chinese military end users.

**Support for Russian and Iranian Military:**

The Entity List reflected concerns about PRC support for Russian and Iranian military-industrial bases. The Commerce Department added 59 Chinese persons for supporting Russian war efforts, supplying Russian military end users with electronics, microelectronics, machine tools, UAV components, and controlled items.

To crack down on evasion, the Commerce Department issued a June 2024 rule adding addresses used by shell companies to the Entity List rather than operating names. Fifteen Chinese addresses involved in "sensitive goods transshipment" to Russia were added. Additionally, several Chinese persons were added for procuring Iran's WMD and UAV program items and Shahed-series drone components for Iranian and Russian militaries.

**Foreign Direct Product Rules Expansion:**

The Biden administration dramatically expanded Foreign Direct Product Rules (FDPRs)—extraterritorial export controls on foreign-made goods produced using U.S. machinery or software—in 2024. The administration applied an FDPR to 105 Chinese entities, slightly more than doubling the roughly 50 average applications in 2022 and 2023. Of these 105 entities, 89 received FDPRs for Russian and Belarusian military end users, underscoring escalating concerns about Chinese companies supplying Russia's military-industrial base. The remaining 16 entities received FDPRs targeting advanced semiconductor capabilities supporting PRC.

### Russia (147 designations)

In 2024, the Commerce Department placed 147 Russian persons on the Entity List, a marked decline from 2023's 207 and significantly fewer than 2022's 374. Despite this decrease, Russia accounted for the second-highest Entity List additions in 2024. Commerce added 140 Russian persons operating within the military-industrial base, procuring military-application items and technology, or violating U.S. export controls and sanctions. Several contributed to Russia's chemical and biological weapons and UAV programs.

**Evasion Network Crackdown:**

The Commerce Department intensified Russian evasion network disruption efforts, prominently focusing on Chinese persons. A total of 135 non-Russian entities were listed for supporting Russia's war effort by violating sanctions and export controls and contributing to the military-industrial base, including the 59 Chinese persons noted above.

An additional 9 Russian persons and 22 non-Russian persons were added for violating export controls and sanctions on both Russia and Iran or contributing to Shahed-series drone production.

**Foreign Direct Product Rules:**

The Commerce Department continued using FDPRs to curb foreign goods and technology flow to Russia. The department broadened the Russia/Belarus-Military End User and Procurement FDPR, making it applicable to "Russian or Belarusian procurement entities" and entities posing "significant risk of involvement in item supply or diversion to procurement networks for Russia's or Belarus's defense industry or intelligence services." Apart from FDPRs, Commerce tightened software and industrial item export restrictions to Russia and Belarus under Export Administration Regulations.

### Other Entity List Additions

The United States used the Entity List complementing financial sanctions and reinforcing sensitive goods and technology flow concerns to nations beyond China and Russia. The Commerce Department added 16 Pakistani persons contributing to ballistic missile, cruise missile, and strategic UAV programs. Additionally, in April, two UAE-based persons were added for violating U.S. export controls on Iran.

The Entity List reinforced efforts targeting human rights violations and antidemocratic activities. Additions included two Chinese persons supplying PRC public security apparatus goods and facilitating ethnic minority abuses. Two Russian persons were added for supplying goods to Burma's military regime, and two others for providing facial recognition software the Russian government uses monitoring and tracking protestors and dissidents—seemingly marking the first time since Russia's Ukraine invasion that Russian persons were Entity-listed specifically for Russia human rights abuses.

Other human rights-related additions targeted Burmese military-affiliated entities and a multinational company supplying Egyptian government surveillance and censorship technology.

## Looking Ahead

In 2025, U.S. sanctions deployment may shift notably under the new Trump administration, which clearly views tariffs as key economic and national security protection tools. Tariffs may supplement or replace sanctions usage.

The United States' Russia sanctions approach will likely shift, with the Trump administration pushing for Ukraine conflict resolution speediness. If first Trump administration trends continue, sanctions targeting PRC and North Korea may increase. A National Security Presidential Memorandum signed in early February directing Treasury to "impose maximum economic pressure" on Tehran suggests similar Iran sanctions escalation.

The second Trump administration may build on both Trump and Biden administration efforts addressing China competition through export controls. U.S. coercive economic statecraft deployment will continue evolving amid escalating geopolitical conflict and worldwide political transitions.

## Terminology & Methodology

### Terminology

"Person" denotes an individual or entity, excluding aircraft and maritime vessels. (Earlier Sanctions by the Numbers series entries prior to 2022 included aircraft and maritime vessels in entity definitions.)

"PRC" references the Chinese state. "China" indicates the entire country, inclusive of nongovernment and nonparty actors.

The SDN List, maintained by Treasury's Office of Foreign Assets Control, represents the most stringent U.S. financial sanction, prohibiting U.S. persons from engaging in designated individual or entity transactions and freezing associated U.S.-based assets.

The Entity List, maintained by Commerce's Bureau of Industry and Security, restricts or prohibits U.S. goods and technology exports to listed persons.

FDPRs represent extraterritorial export controls on foreign-made goods if produced using U.S. software or equipment.

### Methodology

All designations and delistings were drawn from specified OFAC authorities: BALKANS-EO14033, BELARUS-EO14038, BPI-RUSSIA-EO14024, BURMA-EO14014, CAATSA-IRAN, CAATSA-RUSSIA, CAR, CYBER2, DPRK2, DPRK3, DPRK4, DRCONGO, ELECTION-EO13848, GLOMAG, HOSTAGES-EO14078, IFSR, ILLICIT-DRUGS-EO14059, IRAN, IRAN-CON-ARMS-EO, IRAN-EO13846, IRAN-EO13871, IRAN-EO13902, IRGC, IRAN-HR, IRAN-TRA, LEBANON, NICARAGUA, NPWMD, NS-PLC, RUSSIA-EO14024, SDGT, SOMALIA, SOUTH SUDAN, SUDAN-EO14098, SYRIA, SYRIA-CAESAR, SYRIA-EO13894, TCO, UHRPA, UKRAINE-EO13662, VENEZUELA, and WEST-BANK-EO14115.

Data regarding additional sanctions, export controls, and economic measures came from Federal Register Notices, press releases, and BIS Entity List materials. Sanctionable activity information was drawn from OFAC press releases and BIS Federal Register Notices.

Some uncertainty exists quantifying SDN designations by policy priority since government press releases don't always discuss each designation's sanctionable activity. When 100 designations occur but only 50 appear in press releases, the report records "at least" 50 designations for that policy priority.

BIS Federal Register Notice justifications determine Entity List addition reasons. Persons are counted as placed for contributing to China's military modernization if BIS explicitly states they provided military end-user goods and technologies or diverted military-application goods and technologies to China. Persons added for multiple reasons are counted once in total additions but included in each specific reason count.

CNAS's Energy, Economics, and Security Program uses nationality determining SDN List individual categorization, though this may not reflect country residence or origin. For multiple nationalities, OFAC-provided information determines primary nationality. EES uses physical addresses for entity categorization, labeling entities under each country where located.

To avoid double counting in Russia-related SDN sanctions sections, "Russian persons" includes all entities with at least one Russia address. "Non-Russian persons" includes those without Russia addresses. Subsidiaries are listed separately on the Entity List, counted as discrete designations. SDN subsidiaries may be listed separately or captured under OFAC's 50 percent rule, automatically applying SDN restrictions to entities where blocked persons own 50 percent or more, counted as one designation.

Designation overlaps with multiple authorities result in single counts toward total designation numbers but attribution to each authority. Hong Kong and Macau numbers are included within China totals. The West Bank executive order is treated as country-specific authority, targeting specific geographic area activities contrary to U.S. interests.

FDPR applications for any year include entities receiving FDPRs at listing time and entities initially placed in previous years receiving FDPRs in specified years.

State and Treasury Departments coordinate closely on sanctions policy with separate authorities. Any sanctions resulting in SDN List placement were included; those not resulting in SDN placement were excluded.

Since the 2023 Year in Review publication, EES identified and corrected data entry instances and updated SDN List individual nationality attribution methodology. Consequently, some 2022 and 2023 figures differ from prior publication.

The report notes sanctioned entity public statements responding to designation where possible. Gazprombank made no publicly available comment on November 2024 SDN List placement.

For SDN and Entity List datasets included with the report, names, biographical information, addresses, and affiliation and business relationship identification were directly copied from Treasury Recent Action Notices and Commerce Federal Register Notices. "Metadata" tabs detail how authors coded sanctions and export controls. Entity List dataset "Explanations" tabs provide nonexhaustive term clarification.
