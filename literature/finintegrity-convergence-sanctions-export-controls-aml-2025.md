# Navigating the Convergence of Sanctions Evasion, Export Control Evasion, and Money Laundering

**Publisher:** Institute for Financial Integrity / ABA Risk and Compliance Magazine
**Date:** November/December 2025
**URL:** https://finintegrity.org/navigating-the-convergence-of-sanctions-evasion-export-control-evasion-and-money-laundering/

---

## Overview

The article examines how illicit finance networks intersect across three traditionally separate domains: money laundering, sanctions evasion, and export control evasion.

## Core Thesis

Rather than operating as distinct activities, these illicit operations share common methodologies and objectives. The author notes that "the objectives of money launderers, sanctions evaders, and export control evaders are similar: to obscure the real individuals and entities behind financial activity, to disrupt the transactional trail."

## Case Study 1: Russia's Drone Component Procurement

**Background**: Russia's military drones require Western-manufactured components. Research from the Royal United Services Institute found that 71 percent of 450 components in Russian military systems appeared to be U.S.-manufactured. By 2024, approximately $4 billion in export-controlled semiconductor chips had reached Russia from over 6,000 companies.

**Mechanisms**: Illicit procurement networks employ:
- Shell companies as front entities
- Distributors in China, Hong Kong, Turkey, and India
- Transshipment hubs in UAE, Turkey, and Morocco
- Processing of transactions through American financial institutions

**Enforcement Example**: Denis Postovoy, a Russian national residing in Florida, was charged with procurement and illicit export of microelectronics with both civil and military applications to Russia.

**Red Flags Identified by U.S. Departments**:
- Customers with no prior restricted exports suddenly increasing purchases after February 2022
- Customer addresses matching BIS Entity List addresses or sanctioned entities
- Newly established entities connected to previously sanctioned corporate groups
- Supply chains pivoting toward Iran and China as sanctions tighten

## Case Study 2: Iran's UAV Evasion Networks

**Structure**: Iran maintains economic and military ties with Russia and China, sourcing UAV components from China while funding acquisitions through oil smuggling and illicit activities.

**Enforcement Action**: In April 2025, the Department of Justice indicted Iranian nationals Hossein Akbari and Reza Amidi, along with Rah Roshd Company, for conspiring to procure U.S. technology for Iranian UAVs used by Russia against Ukraine.

**Operational Methods**:
- Shell and front companies based in UAE
- Payments processed through U.S. correspondent bank accounts
- Front companies in Hong Kong and China for component procurement
- Sequential company creation when existing entities face sanctions
- Use of third-country exchange houses converting foreign currency to dollars

**Financial Flow Pattern**: Exchange houses convert currency to U.S. dollars through correspondent banking relationships, delivering funds to regime personnel outside Iran to facilitate commodity movement or restricted component procurement.

## Case Study 3: China's High-Performance Chip Acquisition

**Objective**: China seeks Western chips for military modernization, including "to improve the speed and accuracy of its military decision making, planning, and logistics, as well as of its autonomous military systems" and weapons design calculations.

**Scale and Scope**: A 2024 New York Times investigation identified 136 Chinese companies listed as Nvidia partners, with at least 24 having military procurement contracts or connections to BIS Entity List organizations.

**Regulatory Response**: BIS imposed export restrictions in October 2022 and issued updated guidance in May 2025 identifying suspicious patterns.

**Red Flags for Institutions**:
- Customers without prior advanced chip exports suddenly receiving them post-October 2022
- Customers providing residential addresses with no alternative facility locations inconsistent with personal use
- Data centers unable to verify infrastructure capacity for advanced computing operations

## Regulatory Convergence

**Historical Context**: Over the past five years, Treasury and Commerce Departments issued joint guidance documents 15 times on national security issues. Prior to this period, no such joint Treasury-Commerce guidance existed.

**Collaboration Examples**:
- June 28, 2022: FinCEN and BIS joint alert on Russian and Belarusian export control evasion
- August 23, 2024: OFAC and BIS coordinated actions disrupting Russia's supply chain
- January 17, 2025: OFAC and BIS imposed $2.5 million combined penalties against Haas Automation for sanctions and export control violations

Former BIS Assistant Secretary Matt Axelrod stated: "The cooperation with Treasury has been unprecedented. This is especially true with FinCEN. The joint alerts issued...not only contained the key terms, but also provided red flags."

**Exhibit 1** documents 18 joint guidance documents published between July 2020 and May 2025, covering topics from Xinjiang supply chains to Burma business activities, Russia sanctions, Iran UAVs, and Mexico-based cartel oil smuggling.

## Institutional Best Practices

### Organizational Integration

Financial institutions should unify their approach rather than maintaining separate AML, sanctions, and export control compliance silos. Key recommendations include:

**1. Establish "Culture of Cooperation"**: Ensure coordination and communication between specialists across illicit finance domains within compliance departments.

**2. Stakeholder Education**: Educate senior leadership that these networks intersect and that red flags in one area may reveal wider illicit activity. Implement unified organizational structures rather than siloed approaches.

**3. Control Adaptation**: Integrate BIS Entity List addresses and entities into existing screening tools while recognizing functional differences—OFAC-listed entities typically face transaction prohibitions, while BIS-listed entities require nuanced assessment of end-use, end-user, and licensing requirements.

**4. Customized Training**: Provide institution-wide baseline training on AML, sanctions, and export controls, tailored by function. Trade finance teams can identify red flags in trade documents; branch staff can detect structuring attempts by money mules.

**5. Program Integration**: Maintain integrated rather than fragmented responses to emerging threats. When reviewing sanctions advisories, consider implications for AML and export compliance.

**6. Technology Innovation**: Adopt data analytics and forward-looking solutions to identify unusual activity across all illicit finance domains rather than relying solely on backward-looking indicators. Share outcomes across industry.

## Conclusion

The article argues that as illicit actors' methodologies converge and regulators adopt integrated approaches, financial institutions must similarly adapt. Unified compliance programs protecting against money laundering, sanctions evasion, and export control evasion simultaneously serve both financial system integrity and national security interests while reducing operational inefficiencies and gaps in compliance coverage.
