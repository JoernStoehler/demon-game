# International Authority and the Emergency Problematique: IO Empowerment Through Crises

## Abstract

This paper applies the concept of emergency powers to the crisis politics of international organizations (IOs). In the recent past, IOs like the UN Security Council, the WHO, and the EU have reacted to large-scale crises by resorting to assertive governance modes bending the limits of their competence and infringing on the rights of the rule-addressees. In contrast to rational and sociological institutionalist notions of mission creep, this paper submits that this practice constitutes 'authority leaps' which follow a distinct logic of exceptionalism: the expansion of executive discretion in both the horizontal (lowering of checks and balances) and the vertical (reduction of legal protection of subjects) dimension, justified by reference to political necessity. This 'IO exceptionalism', as argued here, represents a class of events which is observable across fundamentally different international institutions and issue areas. It is important not least because emergency politics tend to leave longer-term imprints on a polity's authority structures. This article shows that the emergency powers of IOs have a tendency to normalize and become permanent features of the institution. Thus IO exceptionalism and its ratcheting up represent a mechanism of abrupt but sustainable authority expansion at the level of IOs.

**Keywords:** international organizations; crisis politics; authority; state of emergency; constitutionalism; mission creep; ratchet effect

## Introduction

Over the last two decades, international organizations (IOs) have played politically powerful and legally questionable roles in a diverse set of crises in world politics. In its reaction to the 9/11 terrorist attacks, for example, the United Nations Security Council (UNSC) vastly extended its jurisdiction by empowering itself to act as a global legislator and suspending due process rights of individuals. During the 2002/3 SARS crisis, without prior legal or political authorization to do so, the World Health Organization (WHO) issued travel warnings for affected regions and encroached upon the sovereignty rights of some of its members. Since the outbreak of the euro crisis in 2010, the extra-legal empowerment of the European Central Bank (ECB) to act as a lender of last resort to sovereigns in the Eurozone and to impose austerity measures on recipient states (as part of the so-called Troika) is only the most visible of several crisis-induced shifts in the European Union's (EU) authority structures.

These examples suggest that international crises provide an opportunity window for international organizations to expand their authority beyond the limits set in their founding treaties or customary practice. Yet, what is striking about such authority expansions is not merely their occurrence, but their apparent durability. The UNSC has never rolled back its role as a global legislator. The WHO continues to issue travel warnings. The ECB remains a powerful player in the economic governance of the Eurozone. The same observation holds for numerous other cases. Emergency powers that were introduced as temporary measures to cope with crisis situations tend to become permanent features of international institutions.

## The Logic of IO Exceptionalism

Existing explanations of institutional change at the international level tend to fall into two camps. One group emphasizes the role of material interests or power distributions (rational institutionalists), while another highlights the role of social learning and norm evolution (sociological institutionalists). However, both approaches struggle to fully account for the rapid and dramatic shifts in IO authority that occur during crises.

This paper argues that IO exceptionalism follows a distinct logic that differs from both mission creep and gradual institutional adaptation. It is better understood as a process of authority leaps, where crisis conditions justify the temporary suspension of normal institutional rules and procedures. These authority leaps have distinctive features:

### Horizontal Expansion of Discretion

The first dimension concerns the reduction of internal checks and balances within the IO. During crises, decision-making becomes increasingly concentrated in the hands of executive organs. Oversight bodies lose their ability to constrain executive action. Voting requirements may be relaxed or bypassed. Due process protections are reduced.

### Vertical Reduction of Legal Protections

The second dimension concerns the reduction of protections for those subject to IO decisions. International legal standards for due process, representation, and proportionality may be suspended. Individuals and states lose recourse to normal appellate procedures. Emergency measures are justified as temporary necessities.

## The Ratchet Dynamic

The critical insight is that emergency powers, once granted, rarely disappear. Instead, they tend to normalize over time. Several mechanisms explain this ratcheting:

**Sunk Costs and Institutional Inertia:** Once an institutional structure is in place and bureaucratic routines have developed around it, reverting to the previous arrangement becomes costly. Staff positions created during the emergency period are difficult to eliminate. Procedures that were instituted become embedded in organizational culture.

**Crisis Renewal:** New crises frequently arrive before old emergency measures have been fully rolled back. The UNSC expanded its authority in response to 9/11, then reacted to subsequent security threats with further expansions. Each new crisis reinforces the emergency authority structures rather than requiring their renewal from scratch.

**Audience Costs and Legitimation:** Once emergency measures have been in place for some time, backing away from them signals weakness to both internal and external audiences. IO leadership becomes invested in defending these expanded authorities as legitimate and necessary features of the organization.

**Precedent and Legal Expansion:** Over time, emergency measures acquire legal precedent. They become incorporated into the jurisprudence and practice of the IO. Reverting to previous legal constraints requires explicitly overturning precedent, which carries political costs.

## Counterexample: Potential Rollback Dynamics

However, IO exceptionalism is not inevitable. Under certain conditions, emergency powers may face pushback:

**Political Realignment:** Changes in the distribution of power within the IO (new voting coalitions, leadership turnover) can create pressure to roll back previous expansions. If states that benefited from emergency measures lose political influence, they may lose the ability to protect those measures.

**Delegitimation Events:** If emergency powers produce outcomes that delegitimize them—mass casualties, humanitarian disasters, perceived overreach—publics and member states may demand rollback.

**Normative Contestation:** Rising challengers to the existing order may frame IO exceptionalism as illegitimate and demand restoration of constitutional limits.

**External Pressure:** If a powerful state or coalition of states views IO emergency powers as threatening, they may withdraw from the organization or refuse cooperation, creating incentives for institutional rollback.

## Implications for International Governance

The findings suggest a pessimistic view of institutionalized constraint on international power. Emergency powers, once granted, are durable. This has important implications:

1. **Initial conditions matter enormously.** The emergency measures chosen during the first crisis determine which authority expansions become institutionalized. Decisions made under crisis conditions with incomplete information have long-term structural consequences.

2. **Rollback is possible but costly.** While emergency measures can potentially be reversed, doing so requires either fundamental political realignment or delegitimating events. Incremental reform is unlikely.

3. **Path dependence is severe.** Once institutions develop around emergency authority, alternative institutional arrangements become increasingly expensive to implement.

4. **Democratic accountability is threatened.** As IOs accumulate unchecked emergency authority, the ability of member states and publics to constrain their actions diminishes.

## Conclusion

International crises generate conditions under which IOs can claim extraordinary authority. These emergency powers are justified by reference to political necessity and are typically presented as temporary measures. However, empirical observation reveals that such powers tend to normalize and persist. This ratcheting dynamic represents a mechanism through which international organizations can achieve abrupt but durable expansions of authority.

The emergency problematique thus poses a fundamental challenge to the governance of international organizations. While crises create political space for authority expansion that might be necessary to cope with unprecedented challenges, the tendency of emergency measures to persist raises concerns about democratic accountability, legal constraint, and the long-term distribution of power in international affairs.

The consequences extend beyond individual institutions. As international organizations accumulate emergency powers without equivalent mechanisms for rollback, the overall architecture of international governance becomes increasingly characterized by concentrated, discretionary authority. Understanding the ratchet dynamic of IO exceptionalism is therefore essential for anyone concerned with the future of international law and legitimate global governance.
