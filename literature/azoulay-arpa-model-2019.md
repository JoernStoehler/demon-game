# Funding Breakthrough Research: Promises and Challenges of the "ARPA Model"

**Pierre Azoulay, Erica Fuchs, Anna Goldstein, Michael Kearney**
*NBER Working Paper No. 24674 (June 2018); published in Innovation Policy and the Economy, Volume 19 (2019)*

---

## Abstract

From its 1958 origin in defense, the Advanced Research Projects Agency (ARPA) model for research funding has, in the last two decades, spread to other parts of the US federal government with the goal of developing radically new technologies. In this paper, we propose that the key elements of the ARPA model for research funding are: organizational flexibility on an administrative level, and significant authority given to program directors to design programs, select projects and actively manage projects. We identify the ARPA model's domain as mission-oriented research on nascent S-curves within an inefficient innovation system. Finally, we describe some of the challenges to implementing the ARPA model, and we comment on the role of ARPA in the landscape of research funding approaches.

---

## 1. History of (D)ARPA and Its Younger Siblings

ARPA was founded in 1958 in direct response to Sputnik, charged with preventing further technological surprises. Under its early Directors, the agency developed a flat organizational architecture staffed with top scientists from industry and academia, operating with significant autonomy. Program managers were empowered to build programs independently using tools like "no-year money" (ability to carry unobligated funds forward) and unsolicited proposals.

During the Vietnam War, research was redirected toward shorter-term military applications; ARPA rebranded as DARPA in 1972, and Congress required all military funding to have "direct and apparent relationship to a specific military function." The 1980s and 1990s saw cycles between tighter mission orientation and broader early-stage research focus. Under Director Tony Tether (2001-2008), the agency pivoted further toward applied research with strict go/no-go milestone timelines.

The ARPA model then spread beyond defense:
- **HSARPA** (Homeland Security, 2002)
- **IARPA** (Intelligence community, 2006/2007)
- **ARPA-E** (Department of Energy, authorized 2007, funded 2009) — the most extensively studied ARPA-like agency outside defense

DARPA's attributed successes include the Internet, GPS, the personal computer, the laser, and Microsoft Windows. ARPA-E, being younger, has not yet produced similar outsized wins, making evaluation harder.

## 2. Elements of the ARPA Model

The authors identify four core institutional features that together constitute the ARPA model. They argue these form a **complementary bundle** — mixing and matching elements without the others yields underperformance.

### 2.1 General Organizational Flexibility

Contrary to most federal agencies, ARPA-like agencies have significant structural flexibility:

- **Independence:** ARPA-E's Director reports directly to the Secretary of Energy, bypassing multiple bureaucratic layers within DOE. DARPA similarly reports "to the top."
- **Flat organization:** DARPA has only two levels of internal hierarchy despite a $3B budget. ARPA-E has one: all program directors report to the Deputy Director for Technology.
- **Hiring outside civil service rules:** Both agencies can hire top talent quickly, with competitive salaries, without standard federal hiring constraints. Existing program managers often "tap" promising researchers they meet during their own work.
- **Fixed-term employment:** Program managers serve 3-5 year terms, rotating from academia, industry, or government and returning afterward. This creates constant infusion of new ideas. Alumni networks maintain the agency's reputation and culture.
- **Flexible contracting:** Both agencies have "other transaction authority" (OTA) enabling non-standard agreements — prototyping transactions, technology investment agreements with custom IP terms. This allows DARPA program managers to fund investigators "with millions of dollars practically overnight."

### 2.2 Bottom-Up Program Design

Program managers design their own technical programs based on their assessment of where innovation is needed and where progress is achievable. At ARPA-E:

- A new program director spends up to 18 months of their 36-month contract designing their program — engaging with the research community, hosting stakeholder gatherings, and pitching to ARPA-E leadership.
- Each director typically creates one program: a specific challenge with defined funding ($30M allocated to ~10 teams of $3M each).
- Programs are defined by specific technical targets (e.g., ARPA-E's GRIDS battery program required cost projections below $100/kWh — a threshold that became the industry benchmark).

At DARPA, program managers identify technology directions by simultaneously engaging military liaisons (to understand mission needs) and scientists (to identify feasible technical approaches). Formal working groups (e.g., Information Science and Technology — ISAT) supplement informal one-on-one conversations.

This model is described as **"right-to-left"** (Bonvillian): start with a desired technology outcome, work backward to determine the research needed. Programs also build communities — "you get communities together that don't naturally talk and you give them some latitude and some life, and you push them forward and see what comes out of it."

### 2.3 Discretion in Project Selection

ARPA agencies allow program directors to use their own expert judgment in allocating funds, rather than binding themselves to peer review rankings. This is the sharpest contrast with NSF/NIH-style funding.

The literature documents substantial peer review **bias against novelty**: review panels tend to favor conventional proposals, penalizing the very high-uncertainty ideas that ARPA-like agencies exist to fund. Program directors at DARPA have essentially unlimited discretion; ARPA-E collects peer reviews as informational inputs but does not treat them as binding.

Empirical finding (Goldstein and Kearney 2018a): across ARPA-E's portfolio 2009-2015, approximately half of all funded projects were selected despite being below the hypothetical peer-review cutoff line that would have been used by a ranking-based system. Program directors select across the full distribution of review scores, often choosing projects precisely because reviewers *disagree* — high reviewer disagreement signals technical uncertainty, which correlates with novelty.

Program directors also actively **engineer** research teams: introducing academics to commercial partners, assembling groups with complementary expertise, and sometimes deciding to award contracts to assembled teams rather than the highest-ranked individual applicants.

### 2.4 Active Project Management

Once funded, ARPA projects are not left to researcher autonomy. Program directors remain closely engaged throughout:

- Quarterly progress reviews at both DARPA and ARPA-E
- Milestone-based go/no-go decisions
- Active revision of project budgets, timelines, and milestones in response to quarterly ratings
- Termination of projects that miss major milestones

At ARPA-E, budgets and project lengths were more likely to be expanded than cut (when projects perform well), but projects with poor milestone performance were significantly more likely to be terminated. This active management compensates for the increased portfolio risk taken on through the discretionary selection process.

The key difference from standard grant-making: ARPA-like agencies retain **control rights** over research after funding. Conventional grants (NIH, NSF) give researchers authority to manage their own direction. The ARPA model concentrates the locus of exploration at the program director level, not the individual researcher level.

The authors contrast this with HHMI's model ("funding people, not projects"), which gives performers complete freedom over research direction and has been shown to produce more high-impact publications. The ARPA model trades researcher autonomy for mission coherence and active steering.

### 2.5 The Complementarity Argument

The four elements are proposed to be a complementary bundle — adopting some without others degrades performance:

> "A program that uses individual discretion to select funding recipients may underperform in the absence of active project management."

All four elements depend on highly talented, independent, and empowered program staff. The organizational flexibility is necessary to hire such people; the bottom-up design empowers them to use their judgment; the selection discretion and active management are where that judgment gets expressed. Removing any one element undermines the rationale for the others.

## 3. What is ARPA-ble?

Not all research domains are appropriate for the ARPA model. Three conditions must hold:

### 3.1 Mission Orientation

The research domain must be organized around a technology-related mission with quantifiable goals and trackable sub-goals. The ARPA model is **not** appropriate for:
- Pure basic research ("without thought of practical ends") — no mission to anchor program design
- Research where progress is not measurable (e.g., NIH BRAIN Initiative: "revolutionizing understanding of the human brain" has no practical metrics)

DARPA's mission: technologies critical to national security. ARPA-E's mission (statutory): reduce energy imports, reduce greenhouse gas emissions, increase energy efficiency.

### 3.2 Nascent S-Curves

The technology S-curve framework (Foster 1986): technical performance improves as a sigmoidal function of research effort. Early on the S-curve, returns to effort are small; the middle (inflection point) has highest returns; the tail shows diminishing returns.

**The ARPA model is optimized for nascent S-curves** — technologies that exist but are relatively unexplored and have great potential for improvement. This is distinct from:
- Basic research (no established technology trajectory yet)
- Applied research (extending an existing S-curve through incremental improvement — the model's emphasis on risk and novelty makes it a poor fit)

In Stokes' taxonomy (1997), the ARPA model occupies a portion of "Edison's quadrant" (use-focused, but with immature and novel use cases) — occasionally journeying into "Pasteur's quadrant" (use-inspired basic research) when fundamental science must be advanced to enable the technology.

ARPA-E data supports this characterization: ARPA-E awards have higher patenting rates and equal or higher publication rates than comparable DOE grants, and are more likely to produce both — indicating openness to both invention and discovery at the frontier.

### 3.3 Frictions in the Innovation System

The ARPA model is justified where **private markets chronically underinvest** due to:
- Inability to capture innovation returns (standard market failure)
- Financing constraints for early-stage or hardware-intensive development
- Regulatory constraints blocking commercialization pathways
- National security as a public good (defense underinvestment)
- Collective action problems (no single actor bears enough of the benefit to justify the investment)

In defense: national security is a public good; innovative defense products may face secrecy requirements preventing commercial repurposing. In energy: large capital requirements for demonstration and scale-up; immediate competition with legacy providers upon market entry; no clear commercialization pathway analogous to the pharmaceutical startup-to-acquisition pipeline.

## 4. Challenges for ARPA-like Agencies

### Measuring Long-Term Transformation

ARPA missions aim at industry transformation over decades, but individual projects run 3 years. A technically successful project may not show market impact for 20 years — making short-term congressional evaluation structurally misaligned with the model's actual time horizon.

### One Size Does Not Fit All

ARPA agencies fund large firms, academics, startups, and national labs under a single set of practices, despite radically different incentive structures and organizational contexts. Managing conflicting motivations across performer types is a persistent operational challenge.

### The Dark Side of Discretion

Program directors have great freedom; agency quality therefore depends heavily on the talent of program staff and the Director. The ARPA model "cannot exist under micromanagement by the Director or with under-qualified program staff." The organizational flexibility that enables the model also concentrates institutional risk in a small number of individuals.

### Tension Between Autonomy and Accountability

Fewer checks and balances means program directors are less accountable for the impact of their decisions. The Director is accountable to Congress; program directors are accountable only to the Director. Short tenures mean program directors may move on before their programs' outcomes are known — success or failure of a program does not necessarily affect the program director's next career step.

Informal accountability exists: the reputation of program directors within the technical community serves as a check, and strong ARPA alumni networks maintain standards. But this is a weak substitute for formal accountability mechanisms.

### Building Trust

The program design process depends on researchers sharing ideas — including ongoing, potentially confidential, work. If program managers mishandle these ideas, the agency loses its reputation as a trustworthy partner and the flow of ideas dries up.

### Nurturing Culture with High Turnover

The energetic, risk-taking culture of ARPA-like agencies is a product of their people, but 3-year contracts mean constant turnover. Culture is maintained through: (1) the program creation pitch process, where new directors present to existing directors and administration; (2) project selection justification meetings; (3) alumni networks that maintain institutional reputation.

### Transitioning to Market

The ARPA model focuses on technical S-curves, not diffusion. Moving technologies from research to broad deployment requires a path to commercialization that ARPA agencies are structurally not designed to own. ARPA-E's "tech-to-market" team was assessed as needing "reconceptualization" by the National Academies — the gap between technical success and market adoption remains a persistent failure mode.

## 5. The ARPA Model in Context

The authors situate the ARPA model in a two-dimensional taxonomy of research management:
- **Idea source:** researcher-generated vs. funder-defined mission
- **Execution control:** researcher-autonomous vs. funder-managed

|                         | Researcher autonomy | Funder manages execution |
|-------------------------|---------------------|--------------------------|
| **Researcher generates ideas** | HHMI, many NIH/NSF programs | Venture capital |
| **Funder defines mission** | Gates Foundation, Chan-Zuckerberg | **ARPA model** |

The ARPA model sits in the bottom-right: funder defines the mission, funder actively manages execution. The paper argues for a diverse innovation ecosystem where all four quadrants are populated — ARPA-like agencies are complements to, not substitutes for, conventional funding.

## Key Findings and Arguments

1. **The ARPA model has three structural distinctives:** empowered program directors (not peer review committees), bottom-up program design (mission-derived, not discipline-organized), and active milestone management (not set-and-forget grants).

2. **The "right-tail" strategy is institutional, not just financial:** ARPA's tolerance for failure and emphasis on high uncertainty is built into its selection process (discretion over peer review rankings) and management process (milestone-based termination that limits downside). The agency systematically bets on novel ideas that peer review would reject.

3. **ARPA is not replicable in a bureaucracy:** The model depends on hiring exceptional people outside civil service rules, giving them unusual discretion, and protecting them from standard oversight mechanisms. Attempts to graft ARPA-like elements onto conventional agencies without the full bundle will underperform.

4. **The domain constraint is precise:** ARPA-ble = mission with quantifiable targets + nascent S-curve (not basic, not incremental applied) + market failure or friction. The paper explicitly excludes pure basic research and "blue sky" exploration.

5. **Accountability is the unresolved tension:** The model's effectiveness depends on talented program directors with real authority. But short tenures, career independence, and weak formal accountability mechanisms mean institutional quality is fragile and depends on reputation networks rather than structural safeguards.

6. **Evaluation is structurally impossible at short timescales:** Standard innovation metrics (patents, publications, commercialization activity) are misaligned with breakthrough research timescales. Political evaluation cycles conflict with the actual time horizon for transformational impact.

## Relevance to the ISIA Game

The ARPA model is the closest real-world institutional analogue to what the ISIA would need to be to succeed: a small, mission-driven agency with unusual flexibility, empowered experts, and tolerance for failure. The paper's framework surfaces several tensions directly relevant to the game's failure modes:

- **The talent problem:** ISIA's effectiveness depends critically on the quality of its program managers. Under-qualified program staff is not a correctable bureaucratic problem — it is a structural failure mode. Cards about recruitment, brain drain, or political interference with hiring go to the heart of institutional viability.

- **Accountability without accountability:** ISIA program managers have authority to act and 3-5 year terms. They may not be around when their decisions bear fruit or fail. The game's Trust and Leverage bars track the political consequences of accountability gaps — but the institutional design that causes them is invisible.

- **The measurement problem:** ISIA success cannot be measured in the short term. If "no ASI was developed" is the success metric, the counterfactual is unobservable. This creates a structural vulnerability to political delegitimization — the agency cannot demonstrate its value even if it is working.

- **Mission drift under pressure:** The ARPA model's domain constraint is precise. Political pressure to expand ISIA's mandate (to address AI harms below ASI threshold, to support "beneficial" AI development, to take on verification functions it cannot perform) represents classic mission creep that degrades the model's effectiveness.

- **The commercialization gap:** ARPA agencies are not designed to own technology diffusion. ISIA faces an analogous problem: enforcement and verification are not the same as ensuring that the pause is used productively. The "tech-to-market" failure mode maps to an ISIA failure to translate treaty compliance into actual safety progress.
