# Principal-Agent Analysis and International Delegation

## Introduction

The principal-agent (PA) approach has become the dominant approach to the study of delegation in domestic, comparative, and international politics. Put simply, the PA approach draws from rational-choice theories, arguing that instrumentally rational actors (voters or legislators domestically, states internationally) delegate powers to executive and judicial agents systematically in order to lower the transaction costs of policy-making. In doing so, principals tailor the discretion of their agents as a function of several factors including the demand for credible commitments, the demand for policy-relevant information, and the expected gap between the preferences of principals and agents.

Following the initial act of delegation, PA accounts make predictions about the autonomy and influence of agents, typically theorized to vary as a function of the administrative and oversight mechanisms available to principals. The PA approach has been a limited but fruitful middle-range theory, allowing researchers to problematize and generate multiple, testable hypotheses about delegation and agency across a wide array of empirical contexts.

Despite these purported benefits, principal-agent approaches have come under sustained criticism in recent years, particularly in European Union studies and international relations. This paper reviews and engages these critiques to determine which are misplaced and which merit further consideration.

## 1. Red Herrings

The true red herrings are critiques of PA analysis that arise from misunderstanding or mischaracterization of what PA approaches actually argue.

### First Red Herring: Independent Agent Preferences

The critique that international organizations like the European Commission or European Court of Justice are not "mere agents" of their political principals but possess independent preferences and can influence political outcomes. This misunderstands the definition of "agent" in PA analysis: agents are indeed assumed to have independent preferences. PA analysis gives us a theoretical language for problematizing and generating testable hypotheses about the sources and extent of agents' autonomy and influence.

### Second Red Herring: Agent Impact

The assumption that PA analyses are characterized by bias in answering "Do Agents Matter?" - typically assuming that PA negates the possibility of agents enjoying autonomy. This misunderstanding often arises from extrapolating too readily from early work of Garrett (1992) and Garrett and Weingast (1993), which did underestimate agent autonomy. Later work by these scholars retreated from this extreme position and generated more discriminating analyses of agent discretion.

### Third Red Herring: Agent Preferences

The implicit assumption that agents, in efforts to maximize expected utility, will avoid irritating principals and prefer as much discretion as possible. Alter notes that international courts as "Trustee-Agents" may be guided more by professional norms than by principal preferences, sometimes preferring legislative overturning to nakedly political decisions. However, PA analysis can accommodate a range of assumptions about actor preferences.

### Fourth Red Herring: Incomplete Theory

The observation that PA analysis does not provide a complete theory of international courts or monetary policy and is therefore not useful. This is correct: PA analysis is a mid-level theory about the dyadic principal-agent relationship, abstracting away from third parties. Nevertheless, PA analysis provides a useful starting point for theorizing about delegation decisions and subsequent principal-agent relations. The more damning critique would be that PA approaches model the principal-agent relationship incorrectly, not merely that they are limited to modeling a single dyadic relationship.

## 2. Theoretical Disagreements: Two Logics of Delegation?

Giandomenico Majone's work raises a more complex issue: the distinction between two "logics of delegation." One logic is informed by the demand for policy-relevant expertise, in which principals delegate executive functions within relatively constraining control mechanisms. A second logic is guided by credible commitments, in which principals deliberately insulate their agents ("trustees") so that those trustees may implement policies to which principals themselves could not credibly commit. At the extreme, such "fiduciary relationships" may lead to a complete and irrevocable transfer of political property rights (sovereignty).

### Problems with the Agent-Trustee Distinction

Majone's account is theoretically useful in distinguishing two rationales for delegation (informational vs. credibility), but unnecessarily dichotomizes what should be a continuous variable. In empirical reality, Majone's scenario of complete and irrevocable transfer of political property rights is a theoretical limiting condition rarely realized in practice. Even in areas of extensive Treaty-based delegation to EU agents (European Central Bank, European Court of Justice), member-state principals typically specify detailed and constraining mandates, retain appointment powers, and maintain the power to recontract through amendment.

Grant and Keohane (2005) offer a variation, distinguishing "discretionary authorities" (where principals do not expect to direct behavior beyond defining duties) from "instrumental agents" (where ex ante mechanisms direct behavior). However, this distinction also unnecessarily dichotomizes what should be a continuous variable: discretion.

The Federal Reserve and European Central Bank illustrate this point. Both are widely cited as trustees granted fiduciary powers for monetary policy, often despite considerable short-term displeasure among principals. Yet neither enjoys complete and irrevocable property rights transfer, nor do they enjoy the same degree or type of discretion. The Fed has a relatively broad mandate that can be altered by majority Congressional vote; the ECB has a more constraining mandate prioritizing price stability and can be changed only by unanimous member-state agreement. By dichotomously labeling these as "trustees, not agents," we lose sight of the central fact that their discretion is not absolute but variable and subject to systematic analysis.

PA analysis is not irrelevant to such cases. Rather, PA analysis provides a useful starting point for explaining and establishing the sources and potential limits of extraordinary discretion enjoyed by central banks, courts, and other "trustees."

## 3. Empirical Disagreements: Does PA Capture the Causes of International Delegation and Agency?

The most challenging critiques argue that while PA models may be internally consistent and theoretically coherent, they systematically fail to identify correctly either the reasons and conditions under which state principals delegate powers or the subsequent autonomy and influence of international agents. These empirical and theoretical critiques offer alternative accounts—typically sociological institutionalist or constructivist—that may better explain the empirical reality of delegation and agency.

### 3.1 Alternative Accounts of Delegation

PA approaches agree that principals delegate powers primarily to reduce transaction costs of cooperation, identifying a wide range of transaction-cost-reducing functions agents can perform: monitoring compliance; solving incomplete contracting problems; providing technical expertise; providing credible economic regulation or monetary policies; locking-in political agreements; shifting blame for unpopular decisions; and reducing instability through agenda setting.

#### Methodological Critique

The first critique is primarily methodological: it is difficult or impossible to derive reliable measures of abstract variables like "inherent uncertainty" or demand for credible commitments, and that PA hypotheses are either tested badly or are indeed impossible to test. There is merit to this critique. Scholars should derive measures and proxies for such variables carefully and treat empirical findings with caution. However, difficulty in measuring abstract theoretical concepts is not limited to PA analyses but affects a wide range of theories, including constructivist approaches.

#### Sociological Institutionalist Alternative

A more profound critique argues that political principals may delegate powers for reasons other than reducing transaction costs. Sociological institutionalists argue that powers are delegated not (or not primarily) to reduce transaction costs but because such delegation is widely accepted as legitimate or appropriate. Kathleen McNamara questions the transaction-cost justification for independent central banks, arguing that the economics literature is inconclusive regarding purported benefits. Rather, the spread of independent central banks as an organizational form exemplifies normative or mimetic "institutional isomorphism" - institutions adopted and spread not because of functional benefits but because they come to be seen as legitimate or appropriate.

Martha Finnemore offered similar explanations for the widespread adoption of science bureaucracies, prompted by new norms arising from UNESCO in the post-war era, suggesting that functional logics do not explain the timing or universality of specialized organizational forms.

#### Assessment

This is a compelling alternative explanation that PA analysts should not dismiss quickly. However, problems merit mention:

First, theoretically, one can imagine political principals engaging in purely symbolic delegation at low cost and stakes, such as with national science bureaucracies or under radical uncertainty. But such delegation becomes less likely in high-stakes cases like central banks where costs and benefits are clearer.

Second, SI claims about delegation as normative institutional isomorphism are exceedingly difficult to distinguish in practice from PA counterparts, since high normative legitimacy may simply reflect accurate assessment of functional benefits. Distinguishing empirically between PA and SI hypotheses would require finding predicted functional benefits and demonstrating their absence in specific cases. Absent such a decisive test, SI accounts deserve consideration, but there are theoretical reasons expecting purely symbolic delegation to decline as stakes increase, and methodological obstacles to testing such claims exist in PA and SI approaches alike.

### 3.2 Alternative Accounts of Agents' Autonomy

Perhaps most compelling are critiques disputing the PA account of agents' autonomy and influence. These include two key claims: (a) that principals rely on sources of influence other than their powers as principals; and (b) that agents have idiosyncratic preferences and multiple sources of authority not captured by PA analysis.

#### Karen Alter on International Courts

Karen Alter notes that most PA analysts predict courts will receive substantial discretion (necessary for neutral adjudication) while assuming principals retain control tools (reappointment, legislative overturning, recontracting). By contrast, Alter argues that while states do have means of influencing international courts, these arise not from the principal-agent contract but from other modes of state influence: rhetorical and legitimacy politics, legal avenues such as refusing jurisdiction and settling out of court. While not inconsistent with PA theory, such arguments suggest PA theory itself may not be very useful for studying variation in international judicial decision-making across cases or courts.

#### Constructivist Alternatives: Barnett and Finnemore

Michael Barnett and Martha Finnemore offer fundamentally different views about IO-state relationships. They argue that international organizations are bureaucracies, and this shapes their behavior in systematic ways not accounted for by simple agent preferences. IOs as bureaucracies order the world and deal with problems predictably—sometimes dysfunctionally—and understanding IO behavior requires modeling both PA relationships and bureaucratic culture.

Second, they identify four distinct power resources or sources of authority for international organizations:

1. **Delegated authority** - arising from explicit mandate from member-states, conditional on satisfactory performance
2. **Expert authority** - arising from IOs' control of specialized, policy-relevant information
3. **Rational-legal authority** - arising from IOs as bureaucracies and their ability to present themselves as neutral and objective creators of impersonal rules
4. **Moral authority** - arising from IOs' claim to represent community values and interests rather than partial, self-seeking state interests

These sources place IOs both "in authority" by virtue of offices held and make them "an authority" by virtue of expertise, objectivity, neutrality. This authority gives IOs both autonomy and potential to influence other actors.

Third, Barnett and Finnemore argue that IO power is both regulative and constitutive. While PA models focus on regulation through informal rules, they suggest IOs can also constitute the world by defining new categories of problems to be governed and creating new norms, interests, actors, and shared social tasks. This constitutive role may be the most profound, but it is one rationalist theorists have not addressed.

#### Ian Johnstone on the UN Secretary General

Ian Johnstone argues that the UN Secretary General's primary influence arises not from delegated powers (which are meager) but from his privileged position as a member of an international interpretive community and his ability to persuade others through ongoing legal discourse.

#### Assessment

Running through constructivist work is an interrelated set of claims: (1) empirical claims about IO influence; (2) theoretical claims about influence sources (moral authority, constitutive/persuasive powers); and (3) metatheoretical claims about "ontological blindness" of rationalist approaches to important IO influence sources. These metatheoretical claims are troubling only to the extent empirical claims are supported. If moral authority is marginal for IOs or evidence for persuasive/constitutive power is weak, rationalist ontological blindness need not trouble us greatly. Both constructivist and rationalist accounts bear methodological burden of convincing us that effects are significant enough.

Constructivist claims do not lend themselves readily to falsification. The best empirical work offers essentially plausible narratives in which moral authority and persuasion play roles at the margin, providing some leverage to IOs otherwise enjoying little formal power.

Nevertheless, such studies suggest that IO autonomy and influence may rest on sources of authority and causal/constitutive processes to which rationalist accounts are indeed ontologically blind. Examining Barnett and Finnemore's four sources, rationalist PA analyses can model delegated and expert authority effectively and with greater precision; but rational-legal and moral authority lie outside PA analysis and are difficult to reconcile with its core assumptions, as is the prospect of a constitutive IO role.

To the extent such sources of authority and constitutive processes are important in the real world—an empirical question—students of principal-agent relations may need to concede that competing theoretical approaches generate greater leverage on IO influence than PA models.

## Conclusion

While principal-agent approaches have faced sustained and sometimes compelling criticism, many critiques represent misunderstandings ("red herrings") or needless dichotomies (agent-trustee distinctions). The most substantive challenges are empirical: whether PA approaches correctly predict delegation motives and agent autonomy. These questions can be settled through good empirical work rather than metatheoretical debate.

PA analysis remains productive for understanding delegation and agency at the international level, though scholars should recognize its limitations as a mid-level theory and remain open to complementary theoretical approaches addressing sources of IO influence that lie outside rationalist frameworks.
