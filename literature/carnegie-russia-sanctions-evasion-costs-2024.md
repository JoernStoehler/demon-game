# West Seeks to Increase the Costs of Russia Sanctions Evasion

**Author:** Alexander Kolyandr
**Publisher:** Carnegie Endowment for International Peace (Politika)
**Date:** September 20, 2024
**URL:** https://carnegieendowment.org/russia-eurasia/politika/2024/09/russia-eu-sanctions-trade?lang=en

---

## Overview

Western policymakers have shifted strategy regarding Russia sanctions. Rather than attempting to completely halt exports, they now focus on making sanctions evasion prohibitively expensive through increased enforcement and complexity.

## The Problem: Sanctions Leakage

The EU successfully reduced direct exports to Russia to historic lows. "The bloc's exports to Russia in June were worth 2.4 billion euros" (down from much higher pre-war levels). However, third-country intermediaries—particularly Turkey, the United Arab Emirates, and Armenia—have become conduits for Western goods reaching Russia.

## Evidence of Evasion

**Power Generation Equipment:** EU exports to Russia dropped to 30,000 euros in June 2024 from 35 million euros previously. Simultaneously, Turkey increased imports by 42% to 48.6 million euros, while the UAE surged more than fivefold to 54.8 million euros.

**Aircraft Parts:** Russian direct purchases fell to zero, yet the UAE increased imports nearly fifteenfold to 23.6 million euros, and Turkey boosted them by a third to 12.7 million euros.

**Maritime Equipment:** Landlocked Armenia and Kyrgyzstan showed sudden demand spikes. Kyrgyzstan imported nearly 1 million euros of maritime navigation equipment in January 2024; Armenia imported 6.5 million euros.

## EU Response: The 14th Sanctions Package

Implemented at the end of June 2024, these measures require exporters to establish end-buyer identities and include contractual clauses forbidding Russian use. "The new requirements enter force on December 26, 2024," with six-month grace periods for existing contracts.

Private companies now bear responsibility for compliance verification, facing potential court action, license revocation, and fines.

## Strategic Objective

The approach aims to increase transaction costs, create delays, and force reliance on less reliable financial channels—ultimately driving inflation and economic inefficiency in Russia rather than achieving complete export prohibition.
