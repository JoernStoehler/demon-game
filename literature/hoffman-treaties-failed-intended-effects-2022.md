# International Treaties Have Mostly Failed to Produce Their Intended Effects

## Summary

This study presents a comprehensive systematic review and meta-analysis examining the effectiveness of international treaties across multiple policy domains. The research analyzed 224 quantitative impact evaluations to determine whether treaties achieve their stated policy goals.

## Key Findings

### Overall Effectiveness Pattern

The study reveals a stark disparity in treaty success rates:

- **Most treaties fail**: Across environmental, human rights, humanitarian, maritime, and security domains, treaties showed no statistically significant positive effects on average
- **Trade and finance exception**: Treaties focused on trade and finance (NAFTA, WTO agreements) are the only category showing consistent, measurable positive impacts
- **Paradoxical harms**: Some human rights treaties demonstrated net negative effects, including increases in torture in ratifying nations

### Domain Breakdown

**Ineffective domains:**
- Environmental protection
- Human rights enforcement
- Humanitarian crises
- Maritime agreements
- Security cooperation
- Arms control

**Effective domain:**
- International trade
- Finance and economic agreements

## Design Implications

### What Works: Enforcement Mechanisms

The analysis identified enforcement mechanisms as the only modifiable treaty design feature showing promise for improving outcomes in non-trade domains. Treaties with teeth—mechanisms to detect violations and impose consequences—demonstrated measurably better results.

### What Doesn't Work

Contrary to conventional policy wisdom, the following features showed no improvement in treaty effectiveness:

- Transparency mechanisms alone
- Complaint procedures without enforcement
- Oversight bodies without authority
- Dispute resolution without sanctions power

## Why Treaties Fail

The underlying challenge is enforcement. Without credible mechanisms to deter violation and punish non-compliance, nations optimize for appearance of commitment rather than actual policy change. This problem is particularly acute in domains where:

- Compliance is costly
- Violations are hard to detect
- Offenders are powerful actors
- Competing national interests exist

## Implications for Global Governance

The findings challenge the conventional wisdom that negotiating new treaties is an effective governance strategy. The study suggests:

1. **Focus on enforcement design first**: A treaty's wording matters far less than its ability to detect and punish violations
2. **Trade may be special**: Economic interdependence provides natural enforcement leverage (reciprocal sanctions work). This advantage is unavailable in other domains
3. **Alternative mechanisms needed**: For domains where treaties fail, policymakers may need to pursue enforcement through alternative institutions or approaches
4. **Rare exceptions matter**: When enforcement mechanisms do exist (and are credible), they can drive measurable policy change

## Methodology Notes

The study conducted a systematic review of 224 treaty impact evaluations across peer-reviewed literature, gray reports, and evaluation databases. The meta-analysis examined effect sizes and statistical significance patterns to identify which treaty features predict effectiveness.

**Important caveat**: This analysis examines treaties' effectiveness at producing measurable policy outcomes. It does not assess whether treaties establish normative frameworks, signal commitment, or produce other non-measurable political effects.
